Attitude Flying Principle
Speed vs. Attitude · Rate of Climb vs. Power
THEORY
Pitch Attitude Controls Airspeed — Power Controls Rate of Climb
KEY INSIGHT
The aircraft nose attitude (pitch angle relative to horizon) controls airspeed. Throttle power controls vertical speed (rate of climb or descent). These are independent relationships — they must be internalised and flown as a system.

Speed (Airspeed Indicator): Raise the nose → airspeed decreases. Lower the nose → airspeed increases. Trim removes the control column pressure.

Vertical Speed (VSI): Add power → climb rate increases. Reduce power → descent rate increases. The VSI lags behind actual vertical movement — do not judge ROD until airspeed is stable.

Anticipation: The pilot must set nose attitude for the desired speed, then use power to set the vertical speed. Attempting to control speed with power or ROD with attitude leads to oscillation and poor control.

⚠
VSI Lag
The VSI always lags behind the actual rate of change. Never judge vertical speed until airspeed has stabilised at the selected value. Common error: reducing power before seeing ROD on the VSI.
2
Climbs & Descents
Best Rate (VY) and Best Angle (VX) · Descent Attitudes
MANOEUVRE
Climb at Best Rate (VY = 79 KIAS)
  • Apply full power (2700 RPM)
  • Set climbing nose attitude for 79 KIAS on ASI
  • Maintain level wings, look out during climb
  • Establish My controls - my trim and trim for 79 kt
PA28 NOTE
Fixed-pitch propeller: no propeller fine callout. RPM is governed by power lever position and airspeed.
MANOEUVRE
Climb at Best Angle (VX = 63 KIAS)
  • Apply full power, set nose attitude for 63 KIAS
  • Used for low-altitude climbs or obstacle clearance
  • Climb rate is lower than VY but distance over ground is reduced
  • Transition to VY (79 kt) when obstacle is cleared
TECHNIQUE
Descent at Selected Rate of Descent (ROD)

To descend at a selected rate, reduce power and set a descent nose attitude. The nose attitude controls descent speed (airspeed), and power adjustment changes the descent rate. For example:

  • To descend at 500 fpm and 90 kt: set power for ≈500 fpm on VSI, then adjust nose attitude to maintain 90 kt
  • If airspeed increases, raise nose slightly to slow down
  • If descent rate increases, add power to reduce ROD
⚠
CARBURETTOR HEAT
Apply carburettor heat ON when reducing power for descent. Set to COLD before applying full power (climb or go-around).
TECHNIQUE
Level-Off from Climb or Descent

Anticipate the level-off. Begin raising the nose approximately 10% of the vertical speed before reaching target altitude. Example: if climbing at 500 fpm, start level-off 50 ft below target altitude.

  • Gradually raise nose to level flight attitude
  • Adjust power for straight & level as required
  • Trim to remove control column pressure
  • Stabilise wings and heading

Landing Circuit Introduction
Standard circuit legs · visual marks · look-out discipline
SOP
Circuit Legs at EKRK
UPWIND

Climb straight ahead from runway. Look out before and during turn.

CROSSWIND

Turn 90° left. Continue climb or level off at circuit altitude. Maintain lookout.

DOWNWIND

Turn 90° left again. Descend as required. Abeam the threshold, begin descent prep. Final clear — check for traffic on final approach.

BASE

Turn 90° left towards final. Configure for landing as briefed. Complete landing checks.

FINAL

Turn 90° left onto runway heading. Stabilise approach and descend to land.

TECHNIQUE
Standardising Circuit Turns

The landing circuit is a "receipt for a good landing." Standardisation removes variables and improves approach stability. Use pre-selected visual marks for circuit legs rather than relying on heading indicator alone.

  • Select a landmark for abeam threshold on downwind
  • Estimate turn-to-base point visually using ground reference
  • Fly parallel to runway on base and final using visual alignment
  • Apply DG corrections as needed for wind
FLYING NOTE
Maintain standard bank angle during turns (≈15°). During turn, look out BEFORE initiating, look out DURING turn, and maintain pitch attitude — pitch drops in turns if not actively maintained.
4
Common Errors
Lookout · Attitude Flying · Power vs. Attitude
ERRORS
Critical Mistakes to Avoid
  • Forgetting lookout BEFORE and DURING turns: A turn without lookout is a collision hazard. Always clear the turn area before initiating and maintain visual scanning throughout.
  • Forgetting to fly attitude while turning: Pitch attitude drops in bank. Apply slight back pressure to maintain altitude and nose attitude.
  • Not using "seat of the pants" during turns: Slip or skid indicates improper coordination. Use rudder and aileron together to maintain balanced flight.
  • Reversing power vs. attitude corrections: Most critical. Trying to control speed with power (or ROD with attitude) causes hunting and oscillation. Internalise: attitude → speed, power → ROD.
  • Not applying carburettor heat: Forgetting to set carb heat ON before reducing power for descent or OFF before applying power can cause engine roughness or shock cooling.
