Stall Theory
Critical angle of attack · stall speeds · affecting factors
THEORY
The Aerodynamic Stall
DEFINITION
A stall occurs when the critical angle of attack (approximately 15°–18° for most training aircraft including PA28) is exceeded, regardless of airspeed or attitude. Stall speed varies with weight, configuration, and bank angle.

PA28 Stall Speeds:

  • VSO (Stall speed in landing config): 44 KIAS (flaps 25°)
  • VS1 (Stall speed in clean config): 50 KIAS (flaps up)

Factors Affecting Stall Speed:

  • Configuration: Flaps lower stall speed; higher flaps extension = lower stall speed
  • Bank Angle: Steeper bank requires higher airspeed to maintain altitude (stall speed increases with bank)
  • Weight: Heavier aircraft stall at higher airspeed
  • Altitude & Density: Altitude has minor effect on indicated stall speed

Stall Warning: The PA28 has a stall warning horn that activates approximately 5–10 kt above stall. The horn IS the hard warning — react immediately.

Stall Recognition
Pre-stall cues · stall horn · recovery trigger
TECHNIQUE
Pre-Stall Cues and the Stall Horn

Pre-Stall Warnings (in order of occurrence):

  • Decreasing airspeed (ASI showing lower numbers)
  • Increasing back pressure on stick (aircraft becoming harder to hold up)
  • Buffet (wing flutter, vibration through airframe)
  • Sluggish controls (ailerons and elevator become less responsive)
  • Stall warning horn sounding (audible alarm, typically 5–10 kt above stall)
SOP §1.21
Upon hearing the stall warning horn, callout Expected warning. This confirms you recognise the horn as a stall warning. Do NOT panic — plan and execute recovery.
SOP
Stall Exercise Setup — Call Sequence

Before stall exercise:

  • Complete ACSEL check (≥3000 ft AGL, flaps up initially)
  • Callout: Throttle to idle - expect stall warning
  • Reduce throttle to idle, maintain level wings and altitude as airspeed decreases
  • Upon horn: Expected warning
  • Continue descent (aircraft will stall); upon stall: Stall
  • Execute recovery

    Stall Recovery
    CAPA SOP sequence · angle of attack reduction · power & attitude
    MANOEUVRE
    Stall Recovery Sequence (CAPA §1.21)
    RECOVERY CALLOUT
    Stall — callout upon stall indication. Then execute: (1) Reduce angle of attack, (2) Apply power, (3) Level wings, (4) Climb away.
    • Forward pressure on stick: Reduce angle of attack to below critical angle. Set level flight attitude — do NOT bury the nose. Level flight attitude is slightly nose-low of cruise.
    • Full power (max continuous): Apply immediately after reducing angle of attack
    • Wings level with rudder: Use rudder to coordinate. Aileron alone may deepen stall on dropped wing — use coordinated rudder input
    • Climb away at VY: Once stable, establish climb at 79 KIAS
    ⚠
    ATTITUDE CORRECTION
    Set the correct level flight attitude first — slightly nose low of cruise — then transition to climbing attitude. Do NOT just lower nose to horizon. Fly the nose position, not the altimeter.

    Landing Circuit Refinement
    Visual marks · flare technique · height judgment
    TECHNIQUE
    Approach & Flare — Peripheral Vision & Height Judgment

    Maintaining Circuit Standardisation: Use pre-selected visual marks on downwind (abeam threshold) and base legs. This removes DG-hunting and keeps the circuit square.

    Flare Technique: At the runway threshold, gradually raise the nose to reduce descent rate. The key phrase is "maintaining same height" — progressively raise nose as aircraft decelerates, reducing ground closure rate from rapid descent to zero.

    • Use peripheral vision to judge height — not forward vision down the runway
    • Peripheral vision detects rate of ground closure more sensitively than forward view
    • "Judging rate of descent in the flare" — the flare arrests descent gradually until touchdown
    • Pitch attitude controls rate of descent during flare
    LANDING NOTE
    Adjust attitude and trim after extending configuration (flaps) during approach. Forgetting this causes unstable approaches.

    Common Errors
    ACSEL · Stall Recovery · Carburettor Heat · Landing
    ERRORS
    Critical Mistakes to Avoid
    • Forgetting ACSEL check: ACSEL is mandatory before every stall exercise. Skipping or rushing this check is a marked error. Confirm altitude specifically as AGL, not QNH.
    • Confusing indicated altitude with height AGL: ACSEL requires ≥3000 ft AGL. Altitude indicator shows indicated altitude (QNH-referenced). You must confirm your actual height above ground.
    • Lowering nose too far during recovery: Flying "wheel movement" instead of flying nose attitude. Stall recovery requires setting correct level flight attitude first, then climbing attitude.
    • Forgetting carburettor heat ON/OFF: Carb heat ON before reducing power for descent. COLD (OFF) before recovery power or go-around. Engine damage risk if forgotten.
    • Not coordinating stall recovery with rudder: Aileron alone may deepen stall on the dropped wing. Use rudder to coordinate wing levelling.
    • Forgetting to adjust attitude & trim after configuration change: After extending flaps, nose attitude changes. Must re-trim to remove back pressure.
    • Trying to adjust final glidepath with attitude instead of power: Power controls descent rate; attitude controls speed. Common landing error.
    • Not using peripheral vision during flare: Forward vision down runway is poor for height judgment. Use peripheral vision to sense ground closure rate.
