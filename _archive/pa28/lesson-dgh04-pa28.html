Slow Flight — Theory & Characteristics
Critically low airspeed · aircraft responsiveness · buffet & mushing
THEORY
Flight at Critically Low Airspeed
DEFINITION
Slow flight is sustained flight at critically low airspeeds approaching the stall speed. The aircraft becomes less responsive and aerodynamically less stable. Buffet (wing flutter) and mushing (lack of pitch authority) are warning signs of imminent stall.

Approach Configuration: Flaps 25°, 75 KIAS. This is the normal approach config for PA28. At this speed and configuration, the aircraft nose provides the correct approach attitude; power controls glidepath (descent rate).

Attitude Flying Still Applies: Even in slow flight, nose attitude controls airspeed and power controls altitude (vertical speed). The pilot must be willing to apply significant power to maintain altitude at low speeds.

⚠
Reduced Responsiveness
Aircraft controls become sluggish. Larger control inputs are needed. Buffet is a pre-stall cue — the stall is near.
TECHNIQUE
Rated Descents at Approach Speed

Flaps 25°, 75 KIAS approach configuration. Nose attitude provides the correct flight path. To change the descent rate, adjust power — do NOT change attitude.

  • Set power for desired ROD (e.g., 300 fpm)
  • Set nose attitude for 75 KIAS
  • Trim for 75 kt airspeed
  • To increase ROD: reduce power slightly
  • To decrease ROD: add power slightly

Go-Around Procedure
Standard callout · power, flaps & attitude sequence · 400 ft AGL power adjustment
SOP
Standard Go-Around — Callout Sequence
CRITICAL CALLOUT
Going around - Full power - Flaps 1 or less - G/A attitude - Positive ROC - Safe speed - Flaps up
  • Carb heat COLD (OFF before full power)
  • Going around — commit to abort approach
  • Full power — apply maximum available power immediately
  • Flaps 1 or less — reduce flaps at once (do NOT retract fully yet)
  • G/A attitude — set nose to go-around pitch attitude (≈10° above horizon)
  • Positive ROC — verify VSI shows positive climb before further actions
  • Safe speed — confirm airspeed is increasing toward safe speed (typically 60+ KIAS)
  • Flaps up — only after positive ROC and safe speed confirmed
  • At 400 ft AGL: Max continuous power — reduce from full power to max continuous
⚠
CRITICAL SEQUENCE
Items are performed IN ORDER. Power first, THEN flap reduction. Never retract flaps without positive ROC. Carb heat must be COLD before full power.
SOP
Go-Around From Slow Flight (§1.22.2)
ALTERNATE CALLOUT
Going around - Max continuous power - Flaps 1 or less - Safe speed - Flaps UP

Differs from standard G/A: This callout deliberately OMITS "G/A attitude" and "Positive ROC" because in slow flight configuration the aircraft may not immediately provide positive ROC. The priority is power and flap reduction to safe speed.

  • Apply max continuous power immediately (not "full power")
  • Reduce flaps to 1 or less
  • Achieve safe speed before further flap retraction
  • Omit G/A attitude check — focus on speed and configuration
WARNING
Go-Around Carburettor Heat

MUST be COLD (OFF) before applying full power for go-around. Cold before max continuous power as well. Forgetting this is a marked error.

⚠
ENGINE DAMAGE RISK
Applying carb heat (warm) then full power can damage the engine. Carb heat ON is only for glide/reduced power descent.

Common Errors
Attitude Flying · Power Control · Slow Flight Management
ERRORS
Critical Mistakes to Avoid
  • Attempting to control speed primarily through power: Speed is controlled by pitch attitude. Using power to control airspeed leads to oscillation and poor slow flight stability.
  • Attempting to control vertical speed primarily through attitude: Vertical speed (ROD) is controlled by power. Using attitude to control altitude causes uncoordinated flight and loss of speed control.
  • Forgetting ACSEL check: ACSEL is mandatory before ALL airwork. Skipping this check is a critical oversight.
  • Not trimming during slow flight: Trim removes back pressure and allows hands-free flight. Slow flight without trim is tiring and imprecise.
  • Carburettor heat error: Forgetting to set carb heat ON before reducing power, or failing to set it COLD before go-around power.
