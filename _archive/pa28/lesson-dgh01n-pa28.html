Regulatory Basis — Night VFR
Part FCL, SERA and BL 7-100 — Know the rules
Regulation
Day VFR vs. Night VFR Requirements

Night VFR operations have specific requirements under Part FCL and BL 7-100:

  • Runway approach lighting system (RALS) must be serviceable and visible
  • Landing area lighting must be observable and adequate for safe landing
  • Aerodrome must be equipped with serviceable lighting
  • Pilot must be rated and current for night operations
  • Aircraft must have serviceable anti-collision lights (red/white rotating or strobe)
  • Aircraft must have serviceable navigation lights (red/green/white)
  • Aircraft must have serviceable landing light
  • Approach must never be below 2° approach angle (4 × red on PAPI indicates unsafe approach)

BL 5-19 specifies: Night operations only permitted when ceiling ≥1500 ft, visibility ≥5 km, and no precipitation/haze. Review these before every flight.

2
Aerodrome Lighting — Know What You See
Omni-directional vs. unidirectional characteristics
Theory
Light Types and Visibility

Aerodrome lights are either omni-directional or unidirectional. Understanding which is which determines what you see from different angles:

  • Omni-directional lights: Visible from all directions equally (runway edge lights, threshold lights)
  • Unidirectional lights: Visible primarily from one direction (approach lights, RALS, PAPI)
  • PAPI (Precision Approach Path Indicator): White/red lights indicating glidepath — red = too low, white = on/above glidepath
  • Threshold lights: Green on approach end, red on departure end
  • Runway edge lights: White midfield, amber in final 2000 ft, red on threshold

During approach from a non-standard angle (not aligned with runway), unidirectional lights may not be visible. Expect to lose approach lights once you are at a steep descent angle close to the runway.

Optical Illusions at Night
Your eyes deceive you — know the corrections
Critical
Common Night Illusions
👁
Runway Slope Illusion
Upsloping runway: Appears higher than it is — you fly lower than intended. Correction: Trust the PAPI. Reference your approach angle on PAPI, not visual appearance.
👁
Black-Hole Approach Illusion
No visual ground references beyond runway: Runway appears distant and high. Correction: Rely on PAPI and instruments. Maintain power on approach until flare.
👁
Runway Width Illusion
Narrow runway at night: Appears narrower than actual width. Correction: Review runway dimensions on chart before approach. Maintain centerline visually.
👁
Rain/Haze Effect
Rain/moisture degrades lighting visibility: Lights appear farther away. Correction: Reduce descent rate. Expect to be higher than visual perception suggests.

Night Flying Technique
Mix of instrument scan and visual navigation — NOT solely instruments
Technique
Cockpit Lighting Setup

Correct cockpit light level is critical:

Red Light Setting
Use minimum cockpit lighting level necessary to read instruments. Excessive red light degrades visual acuity and night adaptation. Use the rheostat to dim lights until you can just read instruments without strain.

Why: Your eyes adapt to darkness over 20–30 minutes. Bright cockpit lighting destroys night vision. If you need to look outside, you cannot see — the contrast is too great. Set lights low from the start.

Technique
Power-Assisted Landing — The Night Requirement

Night landings are power-assisted. You cannot rely on engine sound vs. power-off approaches. Maintain power until flare.

  • On final approach, maintain gentle descent with POWER — not by reducing power
  • Do not power off until ready to flare
  • Use power to manage descent angle and ensure you are on the correct glidepath
  • Flare using pitch attitude, then reduce power to idle
  • Land with power in — expect longer landing distance than power-off approaches

Why: At night, you cannot judge height visually. Without power, you cannot adjust descent rate if glidepath is incorrect. Power gives you control. Use PAPI to manage glidepath, use power to manage descent rate.

Critical
Pre-Flight — KOL Review

Before every night flight, review the aircraft's Known Operational Limitations (KOL) to verify all required systems are operational:

  • Anti-collision light serviceable (red/white strobe or rotating)
  • Navigation lights serviceable (red/green/white)
  • Landing light serviceable and bright
  • Cockpit lighting system serviceable (able to dim to appropriate level)
  • All flight instruments lit and readable
  • Transponder working (Mode C / altitude reporting)
  • Radio and navigation aids serviceable
⚠
Equipment Failure at Night
If landing light fails, divert to nearest serviceable aerodrome. Do not attempt night landing without landing light. If cockpit lights fail partially, return to day airfield immediately.
5
Engine Failure at Night — Think Ahead
Plan before the flight — this is still a single-engine aircraft
Procedure
Pre-Flight Contingency Planning

Before every night flight, mentally review potential engine failure scenarios:

  • Engine failure immediately after take-off: Land straight ahead (no turn back)
  • Engine failure during climb: Identify nearby terrain and fields to glide to
  • Engine failure at cruise: Identify available landing fields — do you know terrain below?
  • Engine failure on approach: Land straight ahead on runway or divert to backup field
⚠
Night Terrain Awareness
You cannot see terrain at night. Study the chart and know what obstacles/terrain exist below your track. Know where fields exist. This information may save your life if engine fails.
