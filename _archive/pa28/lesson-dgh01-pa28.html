Trim Technique
The most critical skill — incorrect trim creates a cascade of errors
Technique
How Trim Works

Trim relieves elevator and rudder pressure. Apply trim in measured doses of 1 notch, ½ notch, ¼ notch. The critical rule: Hold attitude rock steady while trimming. The ONLY thing changing is stick force, not attitude.

Critical Error
Many students re-trim the aircraft by allowing attitude to change during trim input. This negates the purpose of trim. Lock the attitude and let trim take the load.

Procedure: You are flying level at 80 kt. There is a pull force on the control column. Apply 1 notch of trim. Check the stick force — is it reduced? If yes, apply another ½ notch. Check again. When the stick is neutralised, stop trimming. The aircraft is now flying hands-off.

Demonstration
Ground Demonstration — Feel the Difference

Before flight, demonstrate stick force on the ground:

  • Controls in neutral, find the amount of force needed to move the control column
  • Understand the feel of free stick movement
  • Trim the elevator fully nose-up, feel the pressure required to hold the column forward
  • Trim the elevator fully nose-down, feel the heavy pull required
  • Return trim to neutral and understand the relationship between trim and stick force

This ground demonstration builds intuition for stick force management in flight.

2
Attitude Flying Principle
Nose attitude controls speed · Power controls vertical speed
Theory
Fundamental Relationship

Nose attitude determines airspeed. If you want to fly faster, lower the nose. If you want to fly slower, raise the nose. Power determines vertical speed (climb or descent rate).

Attitude Control
Set nose attitude → measure airspeed on ASI → maintain attitude with crosscheck
Power Control
Set power → measure vertical speed on VSI → maintain power setting

Example: Maintain 80 kt altitude 2000 ft. Attitude is slightly nose-down for 80 kt. Airspeed drops to 75 kt — lower the nose further. Airspeed climbs to 85 kt — raise the nose. The power is set for level flight (no climb or descent). Do not change power to correct speed.

3
Rudder — Primary Directional Control
Aileron is auxiliary — this is opposite to what many students assume
Critical
Role of Aileron vs. Rudder

Rudder is the PRIMARY directional control. Aileron ASSISTS rudder but is not the primary roll input. Many students instinctively use aileron to turn, which is incorrect.

✈
Correct Turn Technique
To turn left: Apply left rudder (primary). Aileron assists by banking the aircraft. Opposite rudder is applied on the opposite pedal to prevent slip/skid. Power and altitude maintain via attitude.

Why this matters: If you use aileron alone without rudder, the aircraft will slip (nose points outside the turn). Passengers feel uncomfortable, and fuel flow is inefficient. Rudder must lead the turn.

The Crosscheck Refined
Drill this technique until it is automatic
Technique
Scanning Discipline

The crosscheck is disciplined visual scanning:

  • Primary focus (95%) on nose attitude — the nose–horizon relationship
  • Brief glance (½ second max) at ONE instrument — altimeter, heading indicator, or airspeed indicator
  • Immediately return focus to nose attitude
  • Process the instrument data and apply corrective inputs to attitude
  • Repeat the cycle every 5–10 seconds

What you are NOT doing: You are not scanning all instruments continuously. You are not staring at the altimeter. You are not making control inputs based on instruments without checking attitude first.

Instructor Note
If you see the student fixating on instruments, close the hood over the bottom instruments and force them to fly on attitude. Rebuild scanning discipline.
Technique
Positive Aircraft Control

Positive control means you set an attitude and MAINTAIN it. Do not allow the aircraft to wander. Once you establish a 2000 ft level attitude, lock that attitude and hold it. Any deviation is corrected immediately with a small nose input.

Technique: Set the nose attitude. Check the altimeter — are you at 2000 ft? If so, maintain that attitude. If altitude is drifting, adjust nose attitude slightly and re-check. Do not wait for large deviations to correct.

5
Grip & Feel
How to hold the control column
Technique
Control Inputs — Light Hand
✋
Grip Rule
Hold the control column with 2–3 fingers only when feeling aircraft responses. A white-knuckle grip masks control feel and prevents you from sensing how the aircraft responds to inputs. Use thumb and two fingers, with a light but deliberate touch.

Why: With a light grip, you can feel when the aircraft is approaching a stall (back pressure increases). You can feel speed changes through elevator trim. A tight grip numbs all this feedback.

6
Common Errors & Recovery
Mistakes and how to correct them
Common Errors
Mistakes on This Lesson
⚠
Overcontrolling
If altitude drifts 50 ft up, do not make large nose-down inputs. Make small, measured inputs (a few degrees of pitch). Overcontrolling causes altitude to oscillate.
⚠
Not Anticipating Level-Off
If climbing at +500 ft/min from 1500 ft to 2000 ft, begin levelling off around 1950 ft. Do not climb to 2000 ft and then discover you are climbing through. Crosscheck continuously to anticipate deviations.
⚠
Not Understanding Trim
Trim is not control input. Trim is pressure relief. If you are holding back pressure on the stick, apply trim to remove that pressure. Do not increase back pressure to trim.
