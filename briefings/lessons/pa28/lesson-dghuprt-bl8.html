Common Errors

01
Spin Theory & Recognition
What a spin is — entry conditions — stabilised spin recognition
Theory Spin Definition & Physics
Definition
A spin is an AGGRAVATED stall where one wing is more deeply stalled than the other, causing autorotation. The aircraft descends nose-low while rotating around the vertical axis. A spin requires BOTH stall AND yaw to enter — it does NOT enter from a properly coordinated stall.

During slow flight, inadvertent rudder input or insufficient coordination with high angle of attack causes one wing to stall more deeply. The stalled wing will drop, the aircraft rolls and yaws toward the more stalled wing and enters autorotation. In the stabilised spin, the aircraft rotates consistently. Look STEADY over the nose — find a fixed point in the direction of rotation to establish rotation direction.

Manoeuvre PARE Recovery Procedure

P — POWER to IDLE | A — AILERONS NEUTRAL | R — RUDDER full opposite to rotation (wait until rotation STOPS before removing rudder) | E — ELEVATOR briskly forward to break stall, then BACK PRESSURE to recover from dive — must have sufficient G, approx. +2G to +4G

  • Identify rotation direction before beginning recovery
  • Reduce power to idle — reduces rolling moment
  • Move ailerons to neutral — aileron use deepens stalled wing
  • Apply full rudder OPPOSITE to rotation direction
  • Hold rudder until rotation clearly stops — do not rush
  • When rotation stops, briskly move elevator forward to break stall
  • After stall break, apply back pressure to recover from dive
  • Recover with appropriate G-load — approx +2G to +4G
  • Once stabilized, apply power and climb away

Controls Transfer & Bail-Out
Critical briefing before flight — communication signals — holding controls — exit procedure
Warning Controls Transfer During Spin — CRITICAL BRIEFING

Transit of controls MUST be fully briefed BEFORE FLIGHT. Student must understand exactly how to signal control transfer and what it means. Holding controls during spin makes instructor's correction very difficult. DO NOT resist the instructor's inputs.

  • "My controls - my trim" — instructor takes full control and will manage trim
  • "Your controls - your trim" — student resumes full control and manages trim
  • Upon handover, student RELEASES all stick and rudder force immediately
  • Acknowledge receipt: "Your controls" when taking control
  • Acknowledge receipt: "My controls" when instructor takes control
  • DO NOT apply back pressure or hold controls while instructor recovers — let instructor work
!
Rigid Control Holding
If student holds controls too firmly during instructor recovery, the instructor may not be able to apply recovery inputs. This is DANGEROUS. All parties must fully understand the transit procedure and execute it immediately without hesitation.
Warning Bail-Out Procedure

Must be fully briefed at the aircraft before flight. Student must know exactly how to exit the aircraft if required.

  • Location of emergency exits and how to open them
  • Canopy release procedure — understand the jettison mechanism
  • Seat belt and harness release — practice before flight
  • When instructor says "Bail out", student must exit immediately
  • No hesitation — follow briefed procedure exactly
SOP Zlin Aircraft Systems (BL8 Designation)

This aircraft is NOT the PA28. The Zlin Z-142 (BL8) has an injection engine, constant-speed propeller, and full aerobatic capability.

  • Student NOT responsible for take-off or landing. Instructor handles all of these.
  • Student does NOT manage propeller control — instructor only
  • Student focuses ENTIRELY on the stall/spin exercises
  • Familiarize with unusual attitudes in this aircraft before high-altitude work
  • Aircraft behaviour at high angles of attack is different from PA28

SOP Training Area & Altitude Requirements
  • Training conducted in designated aerobatic areas: A, D, or E
  • Minimum altitude for spin work: 4500 ft AGL
  • Typical operating altitude: 3000–4000 ft AGL for initial unusual attitude work
  • Altitude clearance: Minimum 2000 ft AGL by end of recovery (per aerobatic rules)
  • Instructor ensures sufficient altitude throughout — student focuses on recovery technique
