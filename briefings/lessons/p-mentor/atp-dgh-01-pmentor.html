Flight
How to conduct this lesson in the air
Flight Trim Technique & Four Basic Manoeuvres

This lesson introduces four fundamental manoeuvres: climbing, descending, turning, and straight-and-level flight. The student should be taught the trim technique in measured doses — 1 notch, ½ notch, ¼ notch — and understand that trim removes stick force, not attitude. The key concept is think aircraft movement, not control input. Introduce the "nose attitude is primary" philosophy throughout. After each manoeuvre, ask: "What are you trying to do?" to ensure the student is thinking in aircraft movement.

Introduce the crosscheck as a deliberate skill — nose attitude (90–95% focus) then a brief scan to altimeter, heading, then back. Remind the student that rudder is the primary yawing control, not aileron. Demonstrate each manoeuvre first, then hand over control.

P-Mentor Note
On the P-Mentor, normal climb speed is 80 KIAS / 2250 RPM. Cruise climb MAP 24" / 2100 RPM. Ensure student is familiar with the EIS (Engine Information System).
Attitude Flying Philosophy
The student must not be allowed to develop the habit of chasing instruments. At this stage, attitude flying is everything. Correct immediately and explain why.
2
Common Errors
Anticipate and address these during DGH 01
Errors Common Mistakes — DGH 01
✕ Concentrating on instruments instead of nose attitude — staring at the altimeter rather than looking outside
✕ Forgetting positive aircraft control — reverting to thinking in control inputs instead of aircraft manoeuvres
✕ Using aileron as the primary control for heading changes — rudder is primary for coordination and yaw
✕ Unmeasured trim doses — applying too much or too little trim, or spinning the trim wheel without purpose
✕ Changing aircraft attitude while trimming — must hold attitude stable before trimming

Briefing
What to cover during the ground brief
Briefing Trim System & Four Basic Manoeuvres

Introduce the concept of trim: the trim system removes the stick (or yoke) force required to hold a given attitude. It does not change the attitude itself. Trim is applied in measured doses — one full notch, then ½ notch, then ¼ notch — and each dose is checked before the next. "Trim for the picture, not for the instrument reading."

Introduce the four basic manoeuvres: climbing, descending, turning, and straight-and-level flight. For each, the student must first set the correct nose attitude, then adjust trim to remove stick force.

⚠ Key Point
Trim removes stick force, not attitude. Attitude first, then trim. If continuous control force is needed, the aircraft is not correctly trimmed.
2
Key Concepts
Core ideas to introduce and reinforce during this lesson
Concept Trim Doses & Crosscheck Discipline
Trim Technique
Trim in measured doses: 1 notch → ½ notch → ¼ notch. Each dose is checked before the next. Stick force = trim needed. Set attitude first, then trim to remove the force.
  • Crosscheck: Attitude → instrument → attitude (never chase the instrument)
  • Nose attitude is the primary flight reference (95% of focus)
  • Rudder is the primary yaw control — feet must be active at all times
  • A correctly trimmed aircraft requires no continuous control force
  • Think aircraft movement, not control input
⚠ Discipline
Do not allow the student to develop the habit of instrument fixation. Brief scan only, then return to nose attitude as primary reference.
Checklist Briefing Coverage Checklist

  • The four basic manoeuvres: climb, descent, turn, level
  • Crosscheck discipline: attitude-first scanning
  • Rudder as primary yaw control
  • Nose attitude as primary flight reference

De-Briefing Post-flight review · assessment questions · progression criteria
1
Post-Flight Review
Questions to guide the de-brief discussion
Debrief De-Brief Discussion Points
1 Trim technique: Can the student demonstrate understanding of measured trim doses? Did they trim in sequence (1 → ½ → ¼)?
2 Attitude flying: Did the student look outside and reference nose attitude as primary? Or were they fixated on instruments?
3 Crosscheck: Was the crosscheck technique attempted — brief instrument check then return to attitude?
4 Rudder use: Was rudder used as the primary yaw control, or did the student rely on aileron for all directional control?
5 Positive control: Did the student demonstrate positive, purposeful control inputs — thinking aircraft movement?
6 Stick force awareness: Did the student recognise the need to trim, and did they use the trim to remove continuous control forces?
2
Progression
Criteria to progress to DGH 02
Progress Ready for DGH 02
✓ Student demonstrates understanding of measured trim technique
✓ Nose attitude is referenced as primary — not instruments
✓ Crosscheck attempted deliberately (attitude → instrument → return)
✓ Rudder used for coordination throughout
✓ Student thinks in aircraft movement terms, not control input terms
Note
Tolerances at this stage are wide (±200 ft, ±30°) — the aim is concept introduction, not precision. Progress as long as student demonstrates the fundamental concepts.
