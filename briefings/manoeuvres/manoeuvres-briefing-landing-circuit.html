05 · Motivation

The landing circuit is the first true test of your ability to manage an aircraft in a multi-phase sequence. Unlike straight climbs or level turns, the circuit demands continuous control input, speed management, and navigation while descending towards a fixed point. Mastering a stable, properly-shaped circuit is the foundation for safe landing technique. Students who fly sloppy circuits often develop poor landing habits: high and fast approaches lead to ballooning, late flare, and hard touchdowns. A disciplined approach to circuit discipline builds the muscle memory and scan patterns essential for safe operations at any aerodrome.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • High and fast on final — airspeed not reduced to target, causing long float on landing
  • Late flap selection — holding flap 1 until base turn, forcing rushed transitions
  • Ballooning in the roundout — pulling back too hard or not managing power smoothly
  • Power not fully off in flare — aircraft sinks with residual power, hard touchdown
  • Not recognising unstabilised approach — continuing descent below 200 ft when criteria not met
  • Poor circuit shape — crosswind leg drifting downwind, widening turns late in circuit
  • Aim point drift — not monitoring position relative to threshold, landing long or short

Standard Circuit
Procedure from initial climb to final approach alignment
SOP PROCEDURE Circuit Execution
    UPWIND / INITIAL CLIMB
  • Rotate at 53 KIAS, establish positive rate of climb
  • Retract flap at 56 kt — DO NOT retract above this speed (risk of sink)
  • Climb at 70 kt (VY best climb speed) — MCP 2250 RPM
  • Turn crosswind at circuit height minus 200 ft, or at field boundary (whichever first)
    • CROSSWIND
  • Turn smoothly to crosswind heading, maintaining positive ROC
  • Continue climb to 1000 ft AAL — aim to level off by halfway across crosswind leg
  • Level off at 1000 ft — transition to 90 kt cruise configuration
  • Set MAP 22–23" for cruise descent on downwind
  • BUMFICH check abeam airfield: carb heat, fuel pump, mixture, flap trim, ignition, controls, harness
    • DOWNWIND
  • Maintain 90 kt, MAP 22–23", 1000 ft AAL — steady descent not required yet
  • Report "downwind" on radio (controlled) or blind call (uncontrolled)
  • Abeam runway threshold: reduce MAP to 12", select Flap 1, begin smooth deceleration to 70 kt
  • Descent commences: aim for gentle descent ~500 ft/min, maintain circuit altitude until base turn
    • BASE
  • Turn base when approximately 45° past runway threshold (adjust for wind)
  • Select Flap 2 on base turn — DO NOT make large MAP changes on base
  • Maintain 70 kt, adjust MAP to maintain steady 500 ft/min descent to intercept final
  • Aim to intercept final glidepath at approximately 600 ft AAL
    • FINAL APPROACH
  • Call "final" once runway in sight, aircraft aligned, approach stabilising
  • Stable approach criteria: 70 kt, Flap 2, descent ~500 ft/min, aligned within 10° heading
  • Aim point: 150–200 m down runway from threshold — monitor drift continuously
  • Reduce power smoothly as entering ground effect (~500 ft AAL) — DO NOT go fully idle until flare
    • ROUNDOUT & FLARE
  • At 20 ft: begin roundout — raise nose progressively, allow descent to slow
  • At 5 ft: close throttle fully — hold attitude, resist pull pressure as aircraft sinks
  • Touchdown: main wheels first — slight tail-low attitude is correct
  • After touchdown: apply brakes gently, maintain centreline with rudder and nosewheel steering
2
Normal Landing Technique
Rounding out, flare, and managing the descent
TECHNIQUE Landing Approach & Touchdown
GOLDEN RULE
Stable approach = constant speed, constant angle, aligned. If any element is wrong, GO AROUND.

Roundout: The roundout is a progressive increase in pitch attitude that slows the descent rate as you approach the runway. Apply back pressure smoothly — never abruptly. Your descent should reduce from ~500 ft/min to ~100 ft/min by 5 ft AGL. The horizon line should rise steadily; watch the runway move down the windscreen rather than fixating on a point.

Flare: As the aircraft reaches ~5 ft, begin to hold the attitude you've established rather than pulling more. The aircraft will settle towards the runway as airspeed decreases. Resist the urge to "keep pulling" — this often causes ballooning (see below). Allow gravity to work; your job is to manage the rate of descent with pitch attitude and, if needed, small power adjustments.

Ballooning: If the aircraft suddenly rises unexpectedly in the flare, DO NOT push the nose down (risk of hard landing). Instead: add a small amount of power (slight throttle advance), then gently ease back to your landing attitude. This allows you to regain descent without forcing the aircraft down.

Instructor Note
If in doubt about any approach — go around. A go-around is ALWAYS the right decision. Attempting to salvage a poor approach below 200 ft risks loss of control and hard landing.

Power-Off Approach
Simulated engine failure and glide management from downwind
THEORY Power-Off Approach Concept

A power-off approach simulates an engine failure in circuit, forcing you to manage descent and glide without power. The procedure starts when you're downwind abeam the runway threshold at 1000 ft AAL. On command from the instructor (or as a continuous exercise), you will close the throttle to idle, establish best glide speed (70 kt — the same as your normal approach speed), and manage flaps carefully to avoid undershooting or overshooting the runway.

Best Glide: The P2002 best glide speed is 70 kt — conveniently the same as your normal approach speed. This simplifies your workload. At idle, your descent rate will be approximately 500–600 ft/min, which is slightly steeper than a normal descent but very manageable if you plan ahead.

Flap Management: Flaps act as airbrakes in a power-off descent. You have no engine power to trade for lift, so flap usage is critical. If you're low on the glide path and at risk of undershooting, REDUCE flap (e.g., from Flap 2 to Flap 1) to decrease descent rate. If you're high and overshooting, INCREASE flap slowly to increase descent. If you misjudge badly and cannot reach the runway, you have one more tool: the forward slip (covered separately).

Aim Point: Aim to land in the first third of the runway — approximately 400–600 m from threshold. This gives you margin if you're slightly high on the glide path.

Circuit Modification: Depending on your starting altitude and wind, you may need to tighten your circuit (smaller turns, steeper descent). Discuss the planned modifications with the instructor before entering downwind.

4
Go-Around Procedure
Abort approach and climb away safely
PROCEDURE Go-Around Execution

CAPA callout sequence per SOP §1.20 — spoken aloud as each action is performed. Power application: prop full forward first, then throttle (right to left, smooth and gentle).

"Going around - Full power" – Initial combined call. Prop full forward → throttle full forward. Begin rotating nose toward G/A attitude.
"Flaps 1 or less" – Select Flap 1. Never retract directly 2 → 0 at low speed and altitude.
"G/A attitude" – Set and verify go-around pitch attitude. Accelerate through Vx 56 KIAS maintaining positive ROC.
"Positive ROC" – VSI confirms climb. Aircraft ascending, not descending.
"Safe speed" – Above Vx (56 KIAS). Safe to retract flaps — do not let nose drop.
"Flaps UP" – Retract flaps fully. Trim back. Accelerate to Vy 70 KIAS.
"Max continuous power" – At 400 ft AGL, set 2250 RPM MCP. Advise ATC; maintain sequence and re-join circuit.
!
CRITICAL — Go-Around Rules
Never attempt to land after the aim point has been missed at low altitude. If the runway remaining is insufficient to land safely after the numbers, a go-around is mandatory. A safe go-around beats a crash landing every time.

Debrief & Assessment
Key criteria to review after flight
DEBRIEF Post-Flight Review Checklist
1
Circuit Shape: Was each leg at the correct altitude and position? Was the circuit symmetrical, or did you drift downwind on crosswind? Were turns coordinated and at appropriate rates?
2
Speed Control: Did you achieve and maintain 90 kt on downwind, 70 kt on final? Did you reach 63 kt by threshold? Were power and flap changes smooth?
3
Approach Stability: By 200 ft AAL, was the approach stable (speed, descent, alignment)? If not, did you recognise it early and go around, or continue and attempt a salvage?
4
Roundout Height & Quality: Did you begin the roundout at approximately 20 ft? Was it progressive and smooth, or abrupt and jerky? Did the descent rate reduce smoothly?
5
Touchdown Quality: Did the main wheels touch down first (correct), or was the landing flat three-point? Was the landing smooth or hard? Did you maintain centreline?
6
Power-Off Approach (if practised): Did you manage the glide path correctly? Was your descent rate appropriate? Did you use flap changes correctly to adjust descent? Did you land in the target zone?
7
Go-Around (if practised): Was power applied promptly and smoothly? Did you transition to climb attitude correctly? Was flap retraction timed appropriately?
8
Debrief Separation: Normal approach and power-off approach should be debriefed separately, with clear discussion of what worked and what needs improvement in each scenario.
⚠
Debrief Focus Areas
Emphasise consistency over perfection. A slightly high landing with smooth roundout is better than a low, hard landing with good touch-down alignment. Discuss recurring errors and patterns — if you consistently land long, examine why (high and fast entry, late flare management, etc.) rather than treating each landing as isolated.
NEXT STEPS Progression & Consolidation

Once you have completed a minimum of 3 consistent circuits and demonstrated a normal landing within tolerances, the next lesson will introduce crosswind landings and wind correction during approach and landing. If you struggled with any of today's circuits, consolidate the current lesson with a second flight focused on circuit repetition before moving forward.

Key areas for independent study before the next flight: review radio phraseology for circuit calls, study the wind correction principle (crab angle), and practise visual estimation of descent rate (aim for gentle, steady descent, not steep dives or level-offs).
