05 · Common Mistakes
  • Forgetting the ACSEL check — or confusing QNH altitude with AGL height
  • Trimming during stall entry — prohibited per SOP
  • Pulling back during recovery — deepens stall and risks spin
  • Rolling wings level before nose down — stalls the lower wing, can cause spin
  • Not applying full power on recovery — insufficient energy to climb
  • Lowering nose too far (below horizon for clean stall) — wastes altitude

01
Aerodynamics of the Stall
Angle of attack · critical AOA · stall speed · load factor
TheoryWhat Causes a Stall
Key Principle
A stall is caused by exceeding the critical angle of attack — not by a minimum speed alone. A stall can occur at any speed if the AOA is high enough.
  • When AOA exceeds ~15–18°: airflow over the upper wing surface separates → lift collapses suddenly
  • VS1 = 50 KIAS (clean configuration, MTOW) — stall speed in straight and level flight
  • VS0 = 45 KIAS (landing configuration — Flap 2, power off, MTOW)
  • Load factor increases stall speed: VS (in turn) = VS1 × √(load factor) — stall occurs at higher IAS in turning flight
  • At 30° bank: load factor 1.15g → stall speed ~54 kt · At 45° bank: 1.41g → stall speed ~59 kt
TheoryStall Warning Signs — Recognition

Stall warnings appear in order of severity. The earlier the recognition, the less altitude is lost in recovery.

  • Buffet: airframe vibration transmitted through controls — earliest physical warning, 5–10 kt above stall
  • Stall horn: activates 5–10 kt above stall speed — brief configuration and expected warnings during ACSEL check
  • Controls feel mushy: large inputs produce small effect — poor control response
  • High pitch attitude: visible on AI — nose well above horizon as speed decays
  • Nose drop: at full stall — may be accompanied by wing drop; autorotation can begin if yaw is present
SOP Note
Brief students that the stall horn will sound during the approach to stall entry and should be expected and ignored — state this explicitly during the ACSEL Configuration brief: "Expect stall warning — ignore."

⚠
Never Trim During Stall Entry
SOP E2R1 prohibits trimming during any stall entry procedure. Back pressure must be held manually throughout the entry so the student can feel the increasing force required as the stall is approached.

03
Stall Entry Procedures
SOP E2R1 §1.22.4 — Select a point on the horizon · set HDG bug · no trim during entry
ManoeuvreEntry Sequence — By Configuration
Before Every Entry
  • Select a prominent point on the horizon ahead — maintain heading throughout entry and recovery using this reference
  • Set HDG bug on the heading indicator to the selected heading
Clean Configuration Entry
  1. Power to idle — maintain heading and wings level with rudder as needed
  2. Raise nose to maintain altitude — allow speed to decay naturally
  3. Do not trim — hold back pressure manually throughout
  4. At first indication (buffet) or at full stall as briefed — apply recovery
Approach / Landing Configuration Entry
  1. Power to MAP 12" — maintain heading and wings level
  2. Select Flap 1 (approach) or Flap 2 (landing) — adjust pitch to maintain altitude
  3. Power to idle — maintain heading and wings level
  4. Raise nose to maintain altitude — hold back pressure, do not trim
  5. At first indication or full stall as briefed — apply recovery
Turning Stall (15° Bank)
  1. Configure as above (clean or flapped) but before setting power idle:
  2. Establish aircraft in 15° bank — then proceed with respective entry procedure
  3. Maintain the 15° bank throughout approach — do not increase bank further
04
CAPA Stall Recovery — Callouts
SOP E2R1 §1.22.4 — Exact sequence with verbal callouts · applies to all stall types
SOPRecovery Sequence — Verbatim CAPA Callouts
Critical Sequence
"Nose down" and "Roll wings level" are initiated simultaneously — without any pause between them. Nose must stay down while rolling or the lower wing may stall, causing a spin.
At Stall Recognition
  1. Announce: "STALL" — announcement only, no control input yet
  2. Call: "Autopilot disengage" — confirm manual control (habit pattern for all aircraft types)
  3. Call: "Nose down" — push forward to reduce AOA. Clean config: slightly below horizon. More flap = further below horizon.
  4. Call simultaneously: "Roll wings level" — use aileron to level wings, coordinating rudder. These two actions happen in direct continuation without pause.
Once No Longer Stalled (airspeed increasing · no buffet · no horn)
  1. Call: "Adjust power" — apply full MCP (2250 RPM) smoothly
  2. Call: "G/A attitude" — raise nose smoothly to go-around attitude. Must achieve VX or above, but not above VY.
  3. If flaps extended: maintain minimum VX and when positive ROC established, call: "Positive ROC — safe speed — flaps up"
  4. Call: "Recover" — climb using VY, throttle full forward, 2250 RPM, level off at initial altitude
⚠
Wing Drop at Stall
If a wing drops at the stall: DO NOT use aileron alone to pick it up. This increases the AOA on the dropping wing and can trigger a spin. The nose-down input must be maintained while rolling. If autorotation has begun, go directly to the spin recovery procedure.

05
Incipient Stall & Accelerated Stall
Most operationally relevant recovery · turning flight stall speed increase
TechniqueIncipient Stall Recovery
  • Recovery initiated at first indication — buffet or horn — before full stall develops
  • Same callout sequence applies: "STALL" → "Autopilot disengage" → "Nose down" + "Roll wings level"
  • Minimum altitude loss — most operationally realistic technique
  • ATP note: students should learn to SET an attitude (level flight), not simply lower the nose arbitrarily
Instructor Note — ATP
"Positive aircraft control is paramount. Students must learn that recovery is setting an attitude — not wheel movement for its own sake."
TheoryAccelerated Stall in Turning Flight
  • At 15° bank: load factor 1.04g → stall speed ~51 kt (entry bank for turning stall)
  • At 30° bank: load factor 1.15g → stall speed ~54 kt (mid-turn awareness)
  • At 45° bank: load factor 1.41g → stall speed ~59 kt
  • Recognition: same as straight stall — buffet, horn, mushy controls
  • Recovery: identical callout sequence — "Nose down" + "Roll wings level" simultaneously
  • Unloading (releasing back pressure) is the first priority — reduces AOA
WarningStall Below 1000 ft AGL
⚠
Unrecoverable Altitude — Stall Near Ground
An unrecovered stall below 1000 ft AGL may not leave sufficient height for recovery. The ACSEL check (minimum 3000 ft AGL) exists precisely to ensure adequate safety margin. Stall awareness training — recognising the approach to stall — is the operational goal. Never practise stalls in the circuit or below circuit height.
06
Debrief Criteria
SOP E2R1 — Assess each stall exercise individually
DebriefPost-Flight Assessment Points
1
Was the ACSEL check completed correctly? Were height, configuration, and expected warnings all briefed verbally?
2
Was trimming avoided during stall entry? Could the student feel the increasing back pressure through the controls?
3
Were CAPA recovery callouts used in correct sequence: "STALL" → "Autopilot disengage" → "Nose down" + "Roll wings level"?
4
Were "nose down" and "roll wings level" applied simultaneously — not sequentially? Was nose held down throughout the roll?
5
Was full MCP (2250 RPM) applied on "Adjust power"? Was G/A attitude set before flap retraction?
6
Clean stall, approach config, and landing config — were all three demonstrated correctly and to tolerances?
7
Incipient recovery — was the student acting on the buffet warning, or waiting for the nose to drop? Buffet recognition is the goal.
