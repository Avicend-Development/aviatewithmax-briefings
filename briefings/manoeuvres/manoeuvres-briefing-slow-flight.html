Why This Exercise Matters
  • Every landing approach is slow-flight — mastering it transfers directly to circuit safety
  • Builds feel for the aircraft near its aerodynamic limits — the foundation of stall awareness
  • Large rudder inputs at low speed build coordinated flight habits that prevent incipient spins
  • Heightened sensitivity to trim discipline reduces workload in the landing flare
!
Common Errors (ATP INT / SOP)
  • Attempting to control airspeed with power instead of pitch attitude
  • Attempting to control altitude with pitch instead of power
  • Concentrating on the ASI — course drifts unnoticed
  • Insufficient rudder — uncoordinated ball, incipient yaw
  • Inaccurate trimming — excessive and continuous stick force

Aerodynamics & the Slow-Flight Regime
What slow flight is, why it matters and the P-Mentor speed picture
Theory What Is Slow Flight?

Slow flight is controlled flight at or near the aircraft's minimum controllable airspeed — approximately 5 kts above the stall speed. If power were suddenly removed, stall indications would appear immediately.

The SOP defines two distinct training situations:

  • Establishment and maintenance of traffic pattern airspeed (simulates the approach environment)
  • Flight at the minimum controllable airspeed — just above the stall, with stall indications audible but no departure from controlled flight
Theory The Governing Principle
Core Rule — Slow Flight
Pitch attitude governs airspeed.
Power setting governs altitude.

At normal cruise speeds the relationship is intuitive — add power and you climb. In slow flight, the aircraft is operating on the back side of the power curve. The drag is so high that maintaining altitude requires significant power, and small pitch changes produce large speed responses. This reversal of normal feel is why slow flight demands accurate trimming and active power management.

Limitation VMC Only

All slow flight and stall exercises are PROHIBITED during IMC. They shall only be performed during VMC with a clearly defined natural horizon.

⚠ No Horizon — No Exercise
If the natural horizon is obscured by haze, cloud or restricted visibility at any point during the exercise, recover immediately and return to clear conditions.

Procedure — Entry, In-Flight & Recovery
SOP §1.22.2 · step-by-step technique and CAPA recovery callout sequence
Procedure Entry — Establishing Slow Flight
Setup
  1. Complete ACSEL check verbally before commencing
  2. Ease throttle back from cruise power — correct heading with rudder as power reduces
  3. As speed decreases, raise the nose progressively to maintain altitude — do not let the aircraft descend
  4. Trim continuously as pitch changes — use 1, ½, ¼ notch doses
  5. Select flap as required for the exercise. Check speed first: "Speed [X] kts — Flaps 1 set"
  6. Adjust power to maintain target altitude — the nose attitude sets the target speed
  7. When established: steady altitude, target airspeed, trimmed hands-free
Instructor Note
Demonstrate with DG and AI covered: student must fly attitude and use rudder — not fixate on the ASI. Once established, freeze the instruments briefly to reinforce attitude flying.
Technique In-Flight — Maintaining Slow Flight

Once established, perform the following exercises in sequence:

Exercises
  • Straight & level — hold altitude ±100 ft, heading ±10° at minimum controllable airspeed
  • Turns — gentle bank (~15°) at slow flight speed. Note large rudder demand. The ball must stay centred — use top rudder to prevent slip/skid. Altitude loss is common; anticipate with power
  • Minimum-speed climb — from slow flight, increase power to MCP while holding attitude — note slow ROC and sluggish controls
  • Minimum-speed glide — reduce to idle and establish glide at minimum speed. Controls become very heavy; large backpressure needed
  • Configuration changes — observe effect of adding/removing flap on speed and trim requirement
⚠
ESP During Airwork
The Electronic Stability & Protection system must be selected OFF before airwork and ON again after recovery, before using any checklist (SOP §1.22).

Technique Trim Discipline & Rudder Use
  • Trim in doses: 1, ½, ¼ notch — never spin the wheel. Assess, trim, assess
  • Rudder is primary yaw control in slow flight — ailerons produce pronounced adverse yaw at low speed and must be assisted by rudder at all times
  • In turns at low speed: apply inside rudder during roll-in, then top (outside) rudder to hold bank and prevent slip. The ball is your reference
  • Slipping (ball out) = not enough inside rudder. Skidding (ball in) = too much rudder. Either condition increases stall speed
After Airwork Returning to Normal Flight

After completing slow-flight exercises, perform recovery from slow flight first before carrying out any checklist. Once accelerated and flaps retracted, select ESP ON, then carry out the AFTER TAKE-OFF checklist and set cruise power.

Exercise Sequence, Debrief & References
Planned exercise flow · debrief questions · study material
Exercise Planned Exercise Sequence
Demonstration (Instructor)
  1. ACSEL check — verbalised in full
  2. Entry to slow flight — clean, flaps up. Show nose position on horizon vs. cruise
  3. Straight and level at minimum controllable airspeed — trimmed hands-free
  4. Add Flap 1, then Flap 2 — observe effect on trim and required power. Call each flap change with speed check
  5. Gentle turn left and right at Flap 1 speed — demonstrate rudder workload and ball discipline
  6. Recovery demonstration: full CAPA callout sequence
Student Practice
  1. Independent ACSEL check and entry to slow flight — clean configuration
  2. Maintain heading ±10°, altitude ±100 ft — instructor may cover DG/AI
  3. Add flaps to landing configuration — student calls speed check for each
  4. Slow-flight turns: 15° bank, 180° arc minimum — ball centred throughout
  5. Minimum-speed climb: MCP — hold attitude — note ROC and sluggish controls
  6. Minimum-speed glide: idle — hold minimum speed attitude — note backpressure required
  7. Student-initiated recovery: full CAPA callout sequence from memory
Note
Slow flight immediately precedes stall exercises in the syllabus. If the student displays good feel and control, the instructor may progress directly to incipient stall awareness within this lesson.
Debrief Debrief Questions
1
What controls airspeed in slow flight — pitch or power? What happens if you try to use power to correct speed?
2
Define the five elements of the ACSEL check. What is the minimum altitude for slow-flight exercises and why?
3
Why is rudder more critical in slow flight than at cruise speed? What does an uncentred ball indicate at low speed?
4
Recite the CAPA slow-flight recovery callout sequence. Why are "G/A attitude" and "Positive ROC" omitted?
5
What is VSO on the P-Mentor? At what speed were you flying slow flight — how many knots above stall?
6
Why must slow flight and stall exercises only be performed in VMC with a clearly defined natural horizon?
7
How did adding flap affect trim requirements and the power needed to maintain altitude?
8
Describe how you should trim in slow flight. Why does spinning the trim wheel in large doses cause problems?
Standard Release Standard
  • Performs ACSEL check correctly and independently
  • Establishes and maintains slow flight within ±100 ft / ±10° heading
  • Controls speed primarily through pitch, altitude through power
  • Ball centred in all phases — active rudder use demonstrated
  • Correctly executes slow-flight recovery with CAPA callout from memory
