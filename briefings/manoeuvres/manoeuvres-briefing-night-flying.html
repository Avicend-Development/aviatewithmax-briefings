05 · Motivation

Night flight removes the visual cues that day pilots rely on unconsciously — the horizon, terrain features, and depth perception all degrade or disappear. The result is a dramatic shift in workload and the introduction of dangerous optical illusions that can deceive even experienced pilots. Mastering night operations means learning to trust instruments over instinct, developing a reliable combined scan, and understanding where visual illusions occur in the circuit so they can be identified and corrected in real time. These techniques are not optional for the night rating — they are survival skills.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Flaring too high on final — dark runway gives illusion of being lower than actual
  • Using pitch to control glidepath on approach instead of power
  • Cockpit lights too bright — destroys dark adaptation, glare on canopy
  • Fixating on instruments only — neglecting external scan for traffic
  • Not verifying PAPI — relying on runway lights alone for glidepath
  • Spatial disorientation: trusting body senses over AI during turns
  • Not briefing engine failure procedure for night (forced landing options)

Environment
Legal definitions · weather minima · medical requirements · go/no-go criteria
Theory Night VFR Operating Conditions & Legal Requirements
▸ Legal Definition of Night
  • Night = period between end of Evening Civil Twilight (ECT) and start of Morning Civil Twilight (MCT)
  • ECT/MCT = sun 6° below horizon — check published tables or ATIS
  • Night rating required for P1 night operations (PPL/LAPL holder)
  • BL 5-19 Class 2 medical must be night-valid — check issue date and restrictions
▸ Weather Minima (Night VFR)
  • Cloudbase: min 1500 ft AGL (higher than day due to terrain awareness)
  • Visibility: min 5 km (statutory NVFR minimum)
  • Wind: calm to light preferred — XWC max 10 kt for initial night circuits
  • Moonlight: not required but aids terrain awareness on initial lessons
▸ Aircraft Requirements
  • Night lighting: nav lights, strobe/beacon, landing light — all serviceable
  • Instruments: compass, AI, ALT, VSI, HI, OAT — all serviceable for night
  • Panel/cockpit lighting: fully functional — map light, instrument flood light
  • Torch: independent (non-aircraft) — required per CAPA SOP §1.22.1
  • Extra fuel: min 45 min reserve — night diversion more complex
SOP Night operations minimum equipment check mandatory before each night flight § 1.22.1
⛔
Night No-Go Criteria
Do NOT depart if: BL 5-19 medical not night-valid · Navigation/strobe lights unserviceable · Cockpit lighting failed · Cloudbase below 1500 ft AGL · Student torch not carried · Engine failure plan not briefed for night (limited forced landing options).
02
Setup
Cockpit lighting management · dark adaptation · pre-flight preparation · departure briefing
Procedure Cockpit Lighting Setup & Dark Adaptation
Key Principle
Dark adaptation takes 20–30 minutes to reach full effectiveness. Protect it throughout the flight — bright lights inside the cockpit are as dangerous as flying without instruments. Set lighting before engine start.
▸ Cockpit Lighting (Before Start)
  1. Set instrument panel flood light to minimum readable level — dim as possible while maintaining legibility.
  2. Set map light to red if available — red light least damaging to dark adaptation.
  3. Set strobe to ON — nav lights ON as per checklist.
  4. Verify external lights functional: ask FI or use external check before boarding.
  5. Student torch: power on, verify functional — place within easy reach.
▸ Dark Adaptation & Eye Scan
  1. Allow 20–30 min dark adaptation before flight where possible. Avoid white light exposure (phones, bright screens).
  2. Use off-centre vision technique at night — rods are located peripherally; looking slightly off-centre of dim objects improves detection.
  3. External scan: use slow, deliberate sweeps — 10° sectors, dwell 2 sec per sector.
  4. Do not rely solely on strobe lights of other aircraft — look for nav light colour (green = right, red = left, white = tail).
SOP Engine failure at night — brief specific landing options and fields visible from runway — include in departure briefing § 1.22.1
FI Note
Brief the student on the night departure plan before boarding. Night engine failure options at EKRK are limited — reinforce that any failure before rotation = abort, after V1 (rotation) and below 500 ft = straight ahead within 45°. Ensure the student can identify lit areas ahead of the runway for reference.

03
Execution
Combined scan technique · optical illusions · night circuit · approach and landing
Theory Optical Illusions & Night Visual Threats
▸ Common Night Illusions
  • Black-hole approach: dark terrain with lit runway threshold — brain perceives aircraft higher than actual, causing pilot to fly too low on approach
  • Upsloping runway: sloped terrain gives illusion of being high — results in under-shooting
  • Downsloping runway: opposite — illusion of being low — results in over-shooting
  • False horizon: ground lights or star fields mistaken for true horizon — can cause unrecognised bank
  • Autokinesis: stationary light appears to move after prolonged staring — perceived as moving traffic
▸ Countermeasures
  • Use PAPI/VASIS — these are the only reliable glide path references at night. Two red + two white = correct path
  • Cross-check altimeter and VSI against visual cues — do not accept visual alone
  • Use power to control glidepath — pitch for attitude, not to "reach the runway"
  • If disoriented: trust AI — not body senses
  • Brief illusions before flight so student can recognise them in real time
SOP PAPI is the mandatory glidepath reference at night — not runway lights alone § 1.22.1
Technique Combined Instrument + Visual Scan (Night Technique)
Key Principle
At night, shift to ~70% instruments / 30% external. The AI is the primary attitude reference. Cross-check every 3–5 seconds: AI → ALT → HI → VSI → external → back to AI. Never leave the AI for more than 5 seconds.
▸ Straight & Level (Night)
  1. Set attitude on AI — wings level, pitch 0–2° up for cruise.
  2. Cross-check ALT: if climbing, slightly reduce pitch. If descending, slightly increase pitch.
  3. Cross-check HI: if off heading, apply gentle bank on AI — max 10° bank for corrections.
  4. Check VSI: should read zero for level flight.
  5. External check: look for horizon where visible, scan for traffic (10° sweeps).
  6. Return to AI within 5 seconds — reset cycle.
▸ Night Circuit & Approach
  1. Join circuit using cockpit instruments for altitude and heading — use circuit calls as normal.
  2. On base: configure aircraft, check PAPI — target 2W/2R (white/red).
  3. On final: use power for glidepath — pitch for speed (70 KIAS). Check PAPI every 5 sec.
  4. Flare: use landing light, aim at start of runway lights. Do NOT flare early — night makes the runway appear closer.
  5. Touch-down: reduce power gently, maintain directional control on centreline with rudder.
⚠️
Spatial Disorientation
If the student reports feeling "tilted" or uncertain of attitude — immediately direct attention to the AI. Call out "Trust the AI" — do not try to use external cues in disorientation. If vestibular illusion suspected: have student confirm AI wings level, then slowly look up externally.
FI Note
Demonstrate the scan cycle verbally on first pass: "AI — level. Altimeter — 1000. Heading indicator — 270. VSI — zero. Outside — clear. Back to AI." Encourage the student to narrate the same sequence until it becomes automatic. Avoid letting the student fixate — intervene early if eyes stop moving.

04
Check
Standards · assessment criteria · debrief guide · progression decision
Standards Performance Standards & Assessment (SOP §1.22.1)
▸ Minimum Acceptable Performance
  • Cockpit lighting set correctly before engine start — no coaching required
  • Maintains Alt ±100 ft, HDG ±10°, IAS +10/−5 kt in S&L flight (after familiarisation)
  • Demonstrates combined scan — not fixating on one instrument
  • Identifies PAPI correctly — adjusts glidepath using power
  • Completes night circuit without safety intervention
  • Names at least 2 night optical illusions and their countermeasures
▸ Progression Decision Criteria
  • Consolidation (repeat): spatial disorientation not managed independently, consistently ignores instruments, approach without PAPI cross-check
  • Progress (continue): tolerances met within 2 circuits, scan technique established, illusions identified before pointed out by FI
  • Solo consideration: only after demonstrated consistent night circuit + approach performance — FI discretion
Debrief Structured Debrief Guide
▸ Key Debrief Questions
1
What did the PAPI show on final and how did you respond? Was your first instinct to use power or pitch?
2
At what point in the circuit did you feel least confident in your attitude awareness? What instrument resolved it?
3
Describe your scan cycle — did it feel automatic, or were you consciously forcing yourself away from one instrument?
4
Did you notice any illusions on approach? Black-hole effect, runway appearing closer than expected?
5
How did cockpit lighting affect your external scan? Was there any reflective glare on the canopy?
▸ Areas to Address if Performance Was Below Standard
  • Instrument fixation: demonstrate "the world is outside, the instruments confirm it" — external check should be deliberate, not absent
  • Glidepath: reinforce power = glidepath, pitch = speed — have student verbalise PAPI indication on every final
  • Spatial disorientation: discuss the vestibular illusion mechanism — the body lags 2–3 seconds behind the aircraft; AI is always ahead of feel
  • Flare height: use landing light as the primary cue — flare when runway surface texture becomes visible
Review End-of-Lesson Knowledge Check
  • What is the legal definition of night in Denmark? When does ECT occur?
  • What medical certificate restriction applies to solo night flying?
  • Describe the black-hole approach illusion and how to counter it
  • What does 2 white + 2 red PAPI indicate?
  • What is the maximum time you should spend away from the AI during a scan cycle?
  • Why is red cockpit lighting preferable to white during night operations?
  • What is your immediate action if you suspect spatial disorientation?
  • What equipment must you carry that is not on the standard day checklist?
