1
Precautionary vs Forced Landing
Understanding the critical difference
THEORY Precautionary Landing
  • Engine is RUNNING — have power
  • Choice of when and where to land (within available range)
  • More time for assessment and approach planning
  • PAN PAN call (urgent, not immediate danger)
  • Can go around and re-assess if approach unsatisfactory
WARNING Forced Landing
  • Engine has FAILED — no power
  • Must land immediately — limited options
  • Time-critical — field selection must be fast
  • MAYDAY call (immediate danger to life)
  • No go-around unless restart attempted
THEORY Common Reasons for Precautionary Landing
  • Deteriorating weather ahead (lowering cloud, reduced visibility)
  • Fuel state — low fuel (urgency, not emergency)
  • Mechanical concern — engine roughness, vibration, unusual sound or smell
  • Medical — pilot incapacitation or passenger illness
  • Navigational uncertainty / lost (fuel/time critical)
  • Night approaching with no night rating
2
Decision to Land
The critical moment
THEORY Key Principle
CORE RULE
When in doubt — land. A precautionary landing on a suitable field is infinitely preferable to a forced landing in unsuitable terrain.
  • Do not delay decision hoping situation improves — it rarely does
  • Once committed: stick to the decision — second-guessing at low altitude is dangerous
3
Field Assessment Pass
The critical low-level inspection
MANOEUVRE Initial Assessment (from circuit height ~1000 ft)
  1. Identify potential field(s) — at least 2 candidates
  2. Assess size, orientation (into wind), approach hazards (trees, wires, buildings)
  3. Note surface colour and texture (crop rows visible = grass/grain; dark = ploughed)
MANOEUVRE Inspection Pass (500 ft AGL, 70–80 kt)
  1. Approach as if landing — fly complete length of field
  2. Assess: surface obstacles (boulders, logs, furrows, livestock, ditches)
  3. Assess: slope (aim into slope if possible)
  4. Assess: approach and overshoot hazards (power lines, fence, trees)
  5. Note any hazards for the actual approach
  6. Apply MCP and climb away for circuit
SOP Assessment pass must be low enough to see surface clearly. 500 ft AGL is the standard assessment height. DO NOT land on first pass.
4
Low Circuit & Landing
Approach, communication, and commitment
MANOEUVRE PAN PAN Call (during or after assessment)
  1. Transmit: "PAN PAN PAN PAN PAN PAN [callsign] [position] [nature of urgency] [intentions]"
  2. Squawk 7600 (comms failure) or 7700 (emergency) as appropriate
MANOEUVRE Low Circuit (300 ft AGL circuit)
  1. Tighter than normal — field is short, approach must be accurate
  2. Downwind at 300 ft AGL, 70–80 kt, close to field
  3. Abeam threshold: Flap 1, reduce to 70 kt
  4. Base: Flap 2, adjust for glidepath
  5. Final: 70 kt, aim point identified from assessment pass
TECHNIQUE Committal Point — 300 ft AGL
  • Below 300 ft: committed to landing — no further assessment
  • If approach unstabilised above 300 ft: go around immediately — engine is running, full power available
  • If approach good: continue, threshold 63 kt, flare, touchdown
CAPA Procedure Go-Around — CAPA Callout Sequence (SOP §1.20)
Key Difference from Forced Landing
In a precautionary landing the engine is running — a go-around is always available above the committal point (300 ft AGL). Multiple passes are normal. Use every go-around to reassess the landing area.

Apply the full CAPA go-around callout sequence per SOP §1.20. Prop full forward first, then throttle (right to left — smooth and gentle).

"Going around - Full power" – Combined initial call. Prop full forward → throttle full forward. Begin rotating nose toward G/A attitude.
"Flaps 1 or less" – Select Flap 1 if extended. Never retract directly from Flap 2 to 0 at low speed and altitude.
"G/A attitude" – Set and verify go-around pitch attitude. Accelerate through Vx 56 KIAS maintaining positive ROC.
"Positive ROC" – Confirm VSI shows climb before retracting flaps further.
"Safe speed" – Above Vx (56 KIAS). Safe to retract flaps — do not let nose drop.
"Flaps UP" – Retract flaps fully. Trim back. Accelerate to Vy 70 KIAS.
"Max continuous power" – At 400 ft AGL, set 2250 RPM MCP. Advise ATC/radio; re-assess field for next pass or departure.
SOP §1.20 Aviate first — communicate after. Do not advise ATC of the go-around until the procedure is complete and the aircraft is under full control.
5
After Landing
Ground procedures and next steps
MANOEUVRE Post-Landing Procedure
  1. Stop aircraft: brakes, engine to idle
  2. Assess aircraft for damage: walk around check
  3. Communicate: advise ATC/FIS of landing location and status
  4. Advise passengers: remain in aircraft until assessed
  5. Seek assistance: contact relevant authority (police/SAR if appropriate)
  6. Do NOT attempt to take off again without engineer sign-off
⚠
Low Fuel
Never plan to arrive with minimum fuel. Minimum fuel quantity for decision to divert is 45 min final reserve (SOP E2R1). If fuel is below final reserve: you are in an emergency — MAYDAY, not PAN PAN.
⚠
Overconfidence
Most precautionary landing accidents occur when pilots assess a field from 1000 ft as 'suitable' without conducting the low inspection pass. A field that looks clear from altitude may have ditches, furrows, livestock, or obstacles invisible from height. ALWAYS conduct the inspection pass.

REFERENCE Lesson Summary

The precautionary landing is a controlled decision to land on a suitable field before circumstances escalate to an emergency. Unlike forced landings, you have the advantage of engine power, which provides flexibility in assessment and approach. The critical success factors are: (1) making the decision promptly without hope that the situation will improve, (2) conducting a thorough low-level inspection pass to identify field hazards, (3) transmitting PAN PAN at the appropriate moment, (4) flying a tight, accurate low circuit, and (5) maintaining discipline through the committal point at 300 ft AGL.

Always err on the side of caution. A precautionary landing in the daylight on a suitable field is infinitely preferable to a forced landing on unsuitable terrain. Do not let overconfidence or desire to reach your destination override sound judgment.
