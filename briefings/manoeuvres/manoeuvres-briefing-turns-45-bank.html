05 · Motivation

The 45° banked turn is the threshold between normal manoeuvring flight and the regime where aerodynamic demands increase significantly. At 45° bank, load factor rises to 1.41 g and stall speed increases by ~19%. Pilots who have not practised steep turns tend to react to the unusual attitude by reducing bank — the wrong response — or by pulling back aggressively, which raises the risk of an accelerated stall. Mastering the steep turn develops the scan discipline, muscle memory, and confidence to manage the aircraft precisely in all phases of flight.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Not adding power on entry — airspeed decays and altitude drops
  • Not applying back pressure — nose drops as bank increases past 30°
  • Allowing bank to exceed 45° — over-banking tendency not managed
  • Ball not centred — using rudder to control yaw rather than aileron/elevator
  • Rolling out too late — no lead on exit heading, overshooting by 20–30°
  • Fixating inside on instruments — losing external horizon reference
  • Left turn: failing to add power early enough — engine torque aggravates descent

Theory Aerodynamics of Steep Turns — Load Factor & Stall Speed
▸ Load Factor at 45° Bank
  • Load factor (n) = 1 / cos(bank angle). At 45°: n = 1 / cos(45°) = 1.41 g
  • At 60° bank: n = 2.0 g — double the normal weight of the aircraft
  • Stall speed increases with load factor: VS(steep) = VS(level) × √n
  • At 45° bank: stall speed ≈ VS × 1.19 — approximately 19% higher than level flight
  • P-Mentor VS ≈ 44 KIAS → steep turn stall speed ≈ 52 KIAS — maintain IAS well above this
SOP Do not exceed VA (manoeuvring speed) during steep turns — check POH for P-Mentor VA § 1.22.4
▸ Operating Environment
  • Minimum altitude: 3000 ft AGL for initial exercises — allows safe recovery
  • Clear of cloud: minimum 1500 ft horizontally — maintain VMC throughout
  • Clear of controlled airspace — check chart for practice area boundaries
  • Light turbulence or less — moderate turbulence can cause structural exceedance at high bank
  • ACSEL check mandatory before each exercise: Height, Airspace, Security, Engine, Location, Lookout
SOP ACSEL check required before all steep turn exercises — no exceptions § 1.22.4
⛔
Steep Turn No-Go Criteria
Do NOT commence if: altitude below 3000 ft AGL · turbulence moderate or above · aircraft not within VA · ACSEL check incomplete · student unable to maintain S&L flight within normal tolerances (prerequisite not met).

Setup
ACSEL check · entry airspeed · entry heading selection · power setting
Procedure ACSEL Check & Entry Setup
Key Principle
Select a prominent visual reference point on the horizon for the entry heading — you will use this same point to identify roll-out. At 45° bank the turn rate is high; a clear reference makes the 10–20° lead instinctive rather than a guess.
▸ ACSEL Check
  1. A — Altitude: minimum height for the exercise (min 3000 ft AGL). Verify safe recovery altitude.
  2. C — Configuration: clean aircraft, no loose items stowed, control surfaces free.
  3. S — Safety: lookout complete, harnesses secure, student briefed.
  4. E — Engine: ROTAX temperatures and pressures green, carb heat check.
  5. L — Location: practice area, clear of CAS, known position near recognisable landmark.
▸ Entry Configuration
  1. Set cruise power (2250 RPM). Establish S&L at 80–90 KIAS — within normal cruise range.
  2. Select entry heading aligned with a visual landmark. Note heading on HI.
  3. Trim for S&L — aircraft should be in trim before entry, not fighting hands-on.
  4. Brief the student: "I will demonstrate. Note the power addition at 30° bank and the back pressure. Watch the ball."
FI Note
Always demonstrate before the student attempts. Left and right steep turns have different power/control demands — demonstrate both. Point out over-banking tendency explicitly during the demonstration: "Notice I'm holding left aileron to prevent the bank increasing past 45°."

03
Execution
Entry · maintaining the turn · over-banking · roll-out technique · left vs. right differences
Procedure Right Turn Entry (SOP §1.22.4)
Key Principle
Entry is a coordinated sequence — aileron to roll, back pressure as bank increases, power added at 30° bank. All three must happen together. Do not wait for altitude to drop before adding back pressure — anticipate the increased lift requirement.
▸ Entry — Right Turn
  1. Apply right aileron and right rudder — begin rolling smoothly.
  2. At 30° bank: add power (approx. +200 RPM or as needed for type) AND begin increasing back pressure simultaneously.
  3. Continue rolling to 45°. Apply opposite (left) aileron as needed to stop roll at 45° — over-banking tendency begins here.
  4. Maintain 45° with reference to AI and visual horizon. Hold back pressure to maintain altitude. Ball centred.
▸ Entry — Left Turn (Difference)
  1. Apply left aileron and left rudder — begin rolling. Add power from the start of the roll (not at 30°) — engine torque and P-factor aggravate left-turning tendency, so power is needed earlier.
  2. Same back pressure technique — apply as bank increases through 30°.
  3. Over-banking tendency also present in left turn — hold right aileron to maintain 45°.
  4. Left turn typically requires slightly more back pressure than right to hold altitude.
SOP Right turn: add power at 30° bank. Left turn: add power at entry start. Both: back pressure increases with bank. § 1.22.4
Technique Maintaining the Turn & Roll-Out
▸ Maintaining 45° Turn
  • Altitude low: increase back pressure slightly — do not reduce bank
  • Altitude high: reduce back pressure slightly — do not increase bank
  • Bank increasing past 45°: apply opposite aileron — this is over-banking tendency and is normal. Do NOT reduce back pressure to correct bank.
  • Ball out: apply rudder in direction of ball displacement — "step on the ball"
  • Scan: AI bank angle → external horizon → ALT → AI → repeat. Do not fixate.
▸ Roll-Out Technique
  • Lead the roll-out by 10–20° before the target heading (half the roll-in bank angle is a reliable rule of thumb)
  • Apply opposite aileron to roll wings level — reduce back pressure simultaneously as bank decreases
  • Reduce power to cruise setting as wings reach level
  • Retrim if needed — aircraft will be slightly nose-high as back pressure is released
  • Call out entry heading when wings level — compare to target
⚠️
Accelerated Stall Risk
Pulling back aggressively to correct altitude loss at 45° bank rapidly approaches the stall speed (VS × 1.19). If airspeed approaches 55 KIAS during the turn — reduce bank first, then recover altitude at lower bank angle. Never pull through a buffet at steep bank angles.
FI Note
Coach the scan verbally: "AI — 45°. Horizon. Altimeter — on target. Back to AI." If the student is altitude-low, say "more back pressure" not "pull up" — "pull up" leads to over-rotation and bank increase. The correction is always pressure on the yoke, not dramatic pitch input.

Check
Assessment standards · debrief guide · progression decision · knowledge questions
Standards Performance Standards (SOP §1.22.4)
▸ Minimum Acceptable Performance
  • ACSEL check completed before each exercise without prompting
  • Bank angle maintained 45° ±5° throughout the turn
  • Altitude maintained ±100 ft entry altitude
  • Ball centred — coordinated flight demonstrated
  • Roll-out within ±10° of entry heading
  • Correctly identifies over-banking tendency and applies opposite aileron
  • States load factor at 45° bank and resulting stall speed increase
▸ Progression Decision
  • Consolidation (repeat): bank exceeds 50° routinely, altitude loss >200 ft, aggressive back pressure near stall, ball consistently displaced, no lead on roll-out
  • Progress (continue): tolerances met on 3 consecutive turns (both directions), ACSEL consistent, entry/exit technique smooth
Debrief Structured Debrief Guide
1
At what bank angle did you add power for the right turn? For the left turn? What is the difference and why?
2
Did you experience over-banking tendency? How did you recognise it and what control input did you use to correct it?
3
Describe what happened to your altitude during the turn — was the trend consistently up, down, or stable?
4
How much heading lead did you use for roll-out? Did you overshoot or undershoot the entry heading?
5
What is the stall speed in a 45° banked turn compared to level flight? How does that affect your minimum IAS during the manoeuvre?
Review End-of-Lesson Knowledge Check
  • What is the load factor at 45° bank? At 60° bank?
  • Why does stall speed increase in a steep turn?
  • What is over-banking tendency and what causes it?
  • Why is left turn entry different from right turn entry?
  • What are the five elements of an ACSEL check?
  • How much heading lead should you apply for roll-out from 45°?
  • If altitude drops in the steep turn, what is the correct corrective input?
