  • Applying aileron against the roll — increases yaw asymmetry and deepens the spin
  • Target fascination — fixating on the ground instead of instrument cross-check
  • Spatial disorientation — misjudging rotation direction; always use instruments
  • Not releasing opposite rudder once rotation stops — delays dive recovery
  • Pulling back sharply before rotation stops — can tighten the spin or cause secondary
  • Failing to complete ACSEL before spin awareness exercises

Aerodynamics of the Spin
How an aggravated stall becomes a spin — phases, physics, and what the pilot sees
Theory From Stall to Spin
Key Principle
An aircraft cannot spin unless it is stalled. A spin is an aggravated stall in which the aircraft autorotates — one wing stalls more deeply than the other due to yaw, producing asymmetric lift and drag that sustains the rotation.

A spin begins when a stalled aircraft is simultaneously yawed. The advancing (outside) wing moves faster through the air, generating slightly more lift — but it is already stalled, so it instead generates more drag. The retreating (inside) wing moves slower, stalls more deeply, and drops. This asymmetry creates autorotation: the nose pitches down and the aircraft rotates about its vertical axis while continuing to descend.

The most common cause in training is an uncoordinated stall — most typically a base-to-final skidding turn with the lower wing held up by aileron. The pilot's instinctive response to a roll (applying opposite aileron) increases yaw and deepens the outside-wing stall, rapidly developing a spin.

Theory Spin Phases
1 — Incipient

0–2 turns. Rotation is developing; control response is still partially effective. Recovery is easiest and most critical here. Pitch attitude oscillates and airspeed is low.

2 — Developed

2+ turns. Rotation is steady. Nose well below horizon. Airspeed stabilised at low value. Standard recovery inputs required.

3 — Flat

High-drag, slow-rotation variant. Nose near horizon. Harder to recover. Associated with aft CG and misuse of aileron.

4 — Inverted

Uncommon in training. Upright recovery rules reversed. Beyond scope of P-Mentor training.

Theory Recognition Cues
Visual
  • Ground rotating below — one direction, steady rate
  • Nose well below horizon
  • Bank angle changing continuously
Instruments
  • ASI: low and reducing (below stall speed)
  • AI: erratic / tumbled in developed spin
  • Turn coordinator: showing continuous turn
  • VSI: high rate of descent
  • Altimeter: unwinding rapidly
Physical
  • Buffet and loss of control effectiveness
  • Disorientation — trust instruments, not senses

Recovery Technique
Standard PARE principles — then CAPA callout sequence from SOP §1.22.7
Theory Standard PARE Recovery — The Principles

The PARE mnemonic describes the aerodynamic purpose of each recovery action. Understanding why each step works is as important as memorising the sequence.

P
POWER — IDLE
Reduces torque and slipstream effects that can sustain rotation. Prevents excessive airspeed build-up during subsequent dive.
A
AILERONS — NEUTRAL
Aileron deflection in a spin increases yaw asymmetry and can deepen or sustain the spin. Neutral reduces asymmetric drag.
R
RUDDER — OPPOSITE
Full rudder opposite to rotation direction reduces yaw rate and allows the deeper-stalled (inside) wing to unstall.
E
ELEVATOR — NOSE DOWN
Forward stick breaks the stall by reducing angle of attack. Applied after rudder — premature elevator input can tighten the spin.
⚠
Aileron Instinct
The instinctive response to a roll is to apply aileron — but in a spin this is exactly wrong. Aileron against the roll increases the yaw causing the spin and deepens it. Ailerons neutral always.
CAPA Procedure CAPA Spin Recovery — SOP Callout Sequence (§1.22.7)
SOP Rule
At CAPA, the standard recovery is performed using the verbal callout sequence below. Callouts are spoken aloud on every exercise. If in doubt about direction — use instruments, not senses.
SPIN — Recognise the spin. State the direction: "Spin — rotating left" or "rotating right." Confirm on turn coordinator. Trust instruments over senses.
Autopilot disengage — Confirm autopilot is off and full manual authority is established.
Power idle — Throttle to idle. Reduces torque and asymmetric thrust effects. (ROTAX 912: move to idle smoothly.)
Aileron neutral — Release aileron pressure and centre the stick laterally. Do not apply corrective aileron. Neutral reduces yaw asymmetry.
Rudder opposite  Nose down — These two actions are applied simultaneously. Full rudder against the direction of rotation (verified on turn coordinator) to stop autorotation. Forward stick to break the stall — reduces angle of attack below critical. Hold until rotation stops.
⚠
Simultaneous — not sequential
Rudder opposite and nose down must be applied at the same time. Applying nose-down before opposite rudder risks deepening the spin. Applying rudder without nose-down delays unstalling the wing.
— wait for rotation to stop —
Rudder neutral — Once rotation has fully stopped, release rudder to neutral. Maintaining opposite rudder into the dive causes a secondary departure. Confirm on turn coordinator: ball and needle centred.
Raise nose — Smooth, positive back-pressure to recover from the ensuing dive. Do not exceed Va (manoeuvring speed). Avoid sharp pulls — G loading risk. Wings level as nose comes up through horizon.
Adjust power — Apply power as appropriate for the desired climb or level-off attitude. Check engine temps before increasing ROTAX power rapidly.
Recover — Establish normal cruise or climb. Complete ACSEL re-check before any further exercise. Note height lost and report to instructor.

In-Flight Exercises
Spin awareness practice on P-Mentor — incipient only, no developed spin
SOP Limitation P-Mentor Training Scope

Per CAPA SOP E2R1, the Tecnam P-Mentor is approved for spin awareness training only. This means the student will experience and recover from the conditions that precede a spin — not a full developed spin entry.

SOP E2R1 Intentional spins are prohibited on all CAPA aircraft except the BL-8 Super Decathlon. All spin training on P-Mentor is limited to incipient-phase awareness. §1.22.7
Exercise Exercise Sequence — Spin Awareness
Setup — Before each exercise

ACSEL check complete. Above 4500 ft AGL. Choose a feature on horizon for heading reference. Set HDG bug.

Exercise 1 — Cross-controlled stall awareness (instructor demonstrates)
  1. Slow to approach of stall with flaps up, ball displaced (skidding turn)
  2. At stall warning: instructor demonstrates tendency to roll/yaw toward lower wing
  3. Immediate recovery: Rudder opposite Nose down simultaneously — do not use aileron against roll
  4. Straight and level recovery, note height lost
Exercise 2 — Student recovery from approaching incipient conditions
  1. Student sets up slow flight in uncoordinated condition (instructor monitoring)
  2. At first onset of autorotation tendency, student applies full CAPA callout sequence
  3. Verify: SPIN → Autopilot disengage → Power idle → Aileron neutral → Rudder opposite Nose down
  4. On rotation stop: Rudder neutral → Raise nose → Adjust power → Recover
Exercise 3 — Repeat with student calling rotation direction
  1. Student must confirm direction of rotation from turn coordinator before applying opposite rudder
  2. Common error: guessing direction from visual impression — always verify on instrument
  3. Repeat until callout sequence is fluent and direction identification is immediate
Instructor Note
Monitor altitude throughout. Intervene at any point the aircraft exceeds the incipient phase. Maintain dual control awareness. Complete re-check above 4500 ft AGL before each repeat. Student should be able to articulate what would happen if aileron were applied against the roll.

Debrief Checklist
Review each item with student after the flight
Debrief Post-Flight Debrief — Spin Awareness
1
Was ACSEL completed verbally above 4500 ft AGL before every exercise?
2
Did the student correctly identify the direction of rotation from the turn coordinator — not from visual impression?
3
Were ailerons kept neutral throughout? Any instinctive aileron application observed?
4
Were Rudder opposite and Nose down applied simultaneously and in full?
5
Was opposite rudder released promptly once rotation stopped — before pulling out of the dive?
6
Was the dive recovery smooth and within Va — no abrupt back-pressure?
7
Could the student recite the full CAPA callout sequence from memory in correct order?
8
Can the student explain why aileron against the roll is the most dangerous instinctive error?
Sign-Off Spin awareness exercise is complete when the student demonstrates: correct ACSEL check, accurate rotation identification, full CAPA callout sequence, and no aileron misuse on three consecutive exercises. §1.22.7
