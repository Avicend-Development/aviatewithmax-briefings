Power + Attitude = Performance
The fundamental relationship in level flight
Theory
Fundamental Principles

The power-attitude-performance relationship is the cornerstone of aircraft control:

  • Attitude sets airspeed: The pitch attitude you maintain determines how much lift is generated relative to drag.
  • Power sets vertical speed: Engine power determines whether you maintain, climb, or descend at that attitude.
  • Configuration changes both: Flaps alter both the drag coefficient and the pitch moment, requiring adjustment to both power and attitude.
Key Principle
To change speed in level flight: change power FIRST, then adjust attitude to maintain altitude. Trim every change.

In-flight examples:

  • Increase power → pitch UP to prevent climb → airspeed increases
  • Decrease power → pitch DOWN to prevent descent → airspeed decreases
  • Extend flaps → aircraft pitches DOWN (negative pitch moment) → power increases needed to maintain altitude
2
Speed Increase — Cruise to Fast
Acceleration in level flight
Manoeuvre
Level Acceleration (Approach → Cruise)
  1. Smoothly increase power toward 75% (2060 RPM)
  2. Allow nose to rise slightly; apply forward pressure to maintain altitude
  3. Airspeed increases toward 100+ kt — monitor ALT stays level
  4. Trim nose-forward to remove pressure; hands-off test
  5. Verify: altitude ±100 ft, ball centred, airspeed stable
3
Speed Decrease — Cruise to Approach
Deceleration with flap transitions
Manoeuvre
Level Deceleration (Cruise → Approach Speed)
  1. Reduce power to MAP 14" (descent power ~1770 RPM)
  2. Raise nose slightly to maintain altitude
  3. Speed reduces toward 90 kt; at 90 kt: select Flap 1 (check VFE1 = 106 kt — safe)
  4. Speed reduces toward 70 kt; at 70 kt: select Flap 2 (check VFE2 = 96 kt — safe)
  5. Increase power to maintain altitude at lower speed with flaps extended
  6. Trim for each flap setting (nose pitch change significant with Flap 2)
SOP
VFE1 = 106 kt · VFE2 = 96 kt — NEVER extend flaps above these speeds

Flap Effects
How configuration changes affect aircraft behaviour
Theory
Configuration Changes in the P-Mentor
Flap 1: Increases lift + moderate drag. Pitch change moderate with slight nose-down tendency. Allows ~5–10 kt speed reduction before requiring power increase.
Flap 2: Significant lift increase + large drag increase. Marked nose-down pitch change (typically 2–3 degrees). Requires substantial power increase to maintain altitude at 70 kt.
Asymmetric flap: If one side fails to deploy — immediate control difficulty. Maintain airspeed, land immediately at nearest airfield.
G-limit reduction: Clean wing: +3.8g. With any flap extended: +1.9g. Avoid steep turns and avoid turbulent conditions with flaps out.
⚠
G-Limit Reduction
With flaps extended: structural limit reduces to +1.9g. Avoid steep turns and turbulent conditions. Retract flaps if encountering rough air.
5
Trim Discipline
Critical workload management technique
Technique
Effective Trim Practice
Trim after EVERY configuration change — no exceptions. After adjusting power or selecting flaps:
  1. Make a small trim adjustment
  2. Release controls completely (hands-off test)
  3. If nose pitches, apply more trim until pitch is neutral
P-Mentor P-factor note: ROTAX engine exhibits slight left-turning tendency at high power. Right rudder trim offset may be needed during cruise climb. Expect this and compensate with right pedal input.
Approach configuration (70 kt, Flap 2): Requires significant nose-down trim compared to cruise — this is normal. The aircraft will feel "settled" when properly trimmed.
Instructor Note
Trim = workload reduction. An untrimmed aircraft fatigues the pilot and degrades situational awareness. This is a foundational skill that directly impacts flight safety and decision-making capacity.
