05 · Motivation

Engine failure is a critical emergency that every pilot must manage with calm, systematic action. The gliding descent is not a panic manoeuvre — it is a controlled, practiced response that preserves altitude, distance, and options. Pilots who have not trained for gliding descent often fixate on restarting the engine at the expense of selecting a landing field; those who do not know best glide speed may sacrifice range through poor speed control. Mastery of this skill builds the confidence and procedure-focused discipline essential to survive an engine failure at altitude.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Not maintaining 70 kt — faster descent feels "safer" but reduces range drastically
  • Forgetting to trim for glide — control pressure builds and altitude control degrades
  • Delaying ABCDE drill — wasting altitude while fumbling with procedures
  • Attempting restart below 1000 ft AGL — insufficient altitude to recover from failure
  • Fixating on engine restart — neglecting field selection and descent planning
  • Adding flap too early — increases drag and reduces range before field is assured
  • Not calculating remaining glide range — misjudging reachability to field

1
Glide Performance
Best glide speed, glide ratio, altitude and range calculations
Theory Best Glide Speed & Glide Ratio

Best glide speed (P-Mentor): 70 KIAS — This speed gives the maximum distance forward for every foot of altitude lost. At this speed, the ratio of lift to drag is optimized.

Glide ratio: Approximately 10:1 (10 feet forward per 1 foot down). Check the AFM for exact figure and any altitude corrections.

Worked example: From 3000 ft AGL, maintaining 70 kt, no wind — glide range ≈ 5 NM. At 2000 ft AGL → ≈ 3.3 NM range.

Headwind impact: Glide range decreases significantly. If facing a 15 kt headwind at 70 kt ground speed, aim for a closer field.

Tailwind impact: Glide range increases, but do not be tempted to venture too far — turn-back risk increases if engine restart fails.

Key Principle
At best glide speed (70 kt), the P-Mentor covers maximum ground. Any deviation above or below 70 kt REDUCES range.
2
Engine Failure Drill — ABCDE
Immediate action, systematic checks, restart procedure
Check ABCDE Procedure
  1. IMMEDIATE ACTION (<10 sec)
  2. A — Airspeed: Lower nose promptly to establish glide attitude. Target 70 KIAS. Trim as nose comes down to relieve control pressure.
  3. B — Best glide: Trim and maintain 70 kt, wings level. Do not exceed 70 kt (faster reduces range).
  4. SYSTEMATIC CHECK (within 30 sec)
  5. C — Cockpit checks: Fuel selector ON correct tank · Fuel valve ON · Throttle full (may already be) · LANE A + LANE B both ON · Primer in and locked · Ignition switch ON
  6. D — Declare: If restart unsuccessful by 1000 ft AGL, transmit PAN PAN or MAYDAY on 121.5 MHz. State position, altitude, field selected, and your intentions (straight-in approach).
  7. E — Emergency landing site: Select suitable field NOW. Begin planning descent profile and circuit approach. Consider wind, terrain, and obstructions.
Instructor Note
Teach students to execute A and B within seconds (before running out of airspeed), then C within 20 seconds. D and E are ongoing but prioritize field selection — relight attempts are secondary.
SOP SOP E2R1 §1.23.1 — Below 1000 ft AGL: no further restart attempts. Full attention to forced landing approach. § 1.23.1
3
Idle Descent vs Glide Descent
Training reality and simulated engine failure
Theory Idle Descent

Idle descent (engine at idle, ~1400 RPM): Engine remains windmilling with propeller drag providing marginal benefit for restart. Engine is "warm" and ready for go-around if field approach is aborted.

True engine failure (power completely off): Propeller windmilling only — slightly more drag than idle descent, marginally worse glide performance (negligible difference in training).

Training practice: Use idle descent (power to idle, not off). Teach best glide speed (70 kt) and ABCDE drill. Engine restart is simulated only above 1000 ft AGL.

4
Effect of Configuration on Glide
Flap management, descent rate, range trade-off
Technique Flap and Configuration

Clean (no flap): Best glide — maximum range. Maintain clean configuration until field is definitely reachable.

Flap 1: Increases drag → shorter glide range. Use only to steepen approach if overshooting field.

Flap 2: Dramatically increases drag → very steep descent. Use when very close to field and overshoot is certain.

Guideline: Keep clean until field is definitely reachable. Extend flaps to fine-tune glide path only after confirming landing is assured.

Instructor Note
Extend flaps only when you are certain of reaching the field. Retracted flaps cannot be used to extend a glide once extended.
5
Glide Turns
Navigation, altitude cost, bank angle, speed
Technique Turning During Descent

Necessary turns: Turn toward selected field or maintain position within landing area. Plan turns to minimize altitude loss.

Bank angle: Use ≤30° bank. Higher bank increases angle of attack, increases drag, and costs more height. A 360° turn at best glide loses 300–500 ft.

Speed in turns: May need a small increase (+5 kt) in bank to maintain effective glide speed and prevent stall. Monitor airspeed carefully.

Altitude awareness: Constantly monitor altitude. If turns consume more altitude than expected, abandon turns and proceed directly to field.

Debrief & Assessment
Evaluation criteria and critical learning points
Debrief Debrief Criteria
1 Was 70 kt established promptly after simulated engine failure (within 5 seconds)?
2 Was trim set correctly for hands-off glide at 70 kt, reducing control pressure?
3 Was ABCDE drill completed in full, within 30 seconds?
4 Was field selection initiated immediately and revised as descent continued?
5 Was position awareness maintained throughout (altitude, glide range, field heading)?
6 Was the 1000 ft AGL restart-abandonment boundary respected?
⚠
CRITICAL SAFETY BRIEF
Engine failure below 500 ft AGL: If engine fails during initial climb (below 500 ft): DO NOT TURN BACK. Maintain best glide straight ahead or gentle turn into wind. A turn-back below 500 ft typically results in a stall/spin at low altitude — statistically fatal. Brief this rule explicitly before every flight.

Learning Points to Reinforce

  • Best glide is a speed, not a manoeuvre. Faster feels better; it reduces range. Teach the counterintuitive principle early.
  • Trim is essential. Untrimmed flight degrades stick force perception and altitude control. Trim immediately after establishing 70 kt.
  • ABCDE is muscle memory. Practice it on every descent, even in normal operations, so it runs automatically in stress.
  • Field selection is step E, not a thought. Teach students to point at the field within 20 seconds and refine continuously.
  • Restart attempts are secondary. Engine failure drills prioritize landing; restart is a bonus, not the objective.
  • Turns cost altitude. Demonstrate a 360° turn and show the height loss. Teach students to plan descents avoiding unnecessary manoeuvers.
Technique Remedial Focus Areas

If speed is not stable at 70 kt: Emphasize pitch control and trim. Review the power/attitude relationship. Perform slow-speed descents to 50 kt then accelerate back to 70 kt to show the natural glide attitude.

If ABCDE takes longer than 30 sec: Simplify checks. Use a flow card. Practice briefing the ABCDE sequence before flight. Consider removing "D" (declare) from the first run if comprehension is borderline; integrate it later once A–B–C–E are automatic.

If field selection is delayed or vague: Conduct a pre-flight "forced landing field" exercise on the map. Identify 3–4 candidate fields within the training area. During descent, ask the student to call out the field name (not just "that one"). Build decision confidence.

If turns consume unexpected altitude: Reduce turn angles and bank. Demonstrate slow turns at 15° bank. Show the difference in sink rate. Discuss risk of stalling in a steep-banked glide turn at low altitude.

Theory Progression & Follow-Up Lessons

Subsequent flights: Repeat gliding descent practice every 4–6 weeks. Add variation: night operations (if approved), different fields, light winds vs. stronger headwinds, different initial altitudes (5000 ft, 2000 ft).

Integration into other manoeuvres: During stall recovery and spiral dives, call out best glide speed. During instrument approaches, brief the glide descent procedure if engine failure occurs during descent.

Advanced skill: Once basic gliding descent is solid, introduce slip manoeuvres (forward slip, side slip) to increase descent rate without adding speed — valuable for overshooting prevention.

Check rides and assessment: Gliding descent is a required element of the emergency procedures check. Ensure student can execute A–B–C–E within 30 seconds and estimate glide range accurately.
