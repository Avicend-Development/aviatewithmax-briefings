1
Attitude Flying
The foundation of straight and level flight
Theory The Attitude Formula
Key Principle
Attitude + Power = Performance — set the attitude, set the power, trim, then verify with instruments
Power + attitude define performance: At 65% power (1975 RPM) with level pitch attitude, the aircraft achieves a cruise of approximately 90–95 kt. The AI is your primary reference — it shows pitch and bank instantly. The altimeter and VSI are lagging instruments; they confirm what you've set, they don't lead. The Heading Indicator shows heading; keep wings level on the AI to maintain track.
2
Reference Points
Using outside references to fly attitude
Technique Horizon & Nose Position
  • Select a prominent feature on the horizon dead ahead as heading reference
  • Note where the cowling/nose sits relative to the horizon for pitch attitude
  • For level flight: horizon bisects AI at mid-point of miniature aircraft wings
  • Use peripheral vision for instrument cross-check while maintaining outside lookout
3
Cross-Check Cycle
Structured instrument scan to maintain control
Check Primary Scan Pattern
Scan sequence (prioritized):
AI (60%) ALT (15%) HI (15%) IAS (10%)
  • Deviation detected on AI → correct immediately (small input) → verify on secondary instrument
  • Never stare at a single instrument for more than 2 seconds
  • Rule of thumb: "Radial scan — always return to the AI"
4
Trim Technique
Removing control pressures for smooth, stable flight
Technique Trim Procedure
  • Set attitude on AI → stabilise airspeed
  • Trim to remove stick/rudder pressure (hands-off test)
  • EVERY power change requires re-trim
  • Note: P-Mentor ROTAX 912 has slight right rudder trim offset in climb
Instrument Note
Trim is not a flight control — it relieves pressure, it does not fly the aircraft. You must always have hand on control to respond to deviations.

Debrief Criteria
What to review after this flight
Debrief Post-Flight Review Checklist
1 Altitude control: Was altitude maintained ±100 ft throughout?
2 Flying method: Was attitude flying used (set attitude → verify) or instrument chasing?
3 Trim: Was trim used effectively after power changes?
4 Heading: Was heading maintained ±10° with reference to ground feature + HI?
5 Lookout: Was outside lookout maintained (70% outside)?
6 Common issues: Late trim, over-banking in corrections, fixation on single instrument?
!
Look Out
Instrument flying must never compromise external lookout. Maintain 70% outside / 30% inside instrument scan ratio.
7
Key Learning Points
Summary of essential concepts
Summary Straight and Level Flight Essentials
Attitude + Power = Performance
The aircraft will do what you set it up to do. Set the attitude and power; trim; verify with instruments.
AI is primary
The attitude indicator shows bank and pitch instantly. Altimeter and VSI are lag instruments — they tell you what happened, not what to do.
Trim every time
After any power change, retrim. This makes the aircraft stable and ready to maintain altitude without hand pressure.
Small inputs
Straight and level requires gentle control inputs. Large corrections lead to chase, instability, and altitude overshoot.
Next Progression Path
  • Once straight and level is solid ±100 ft, ±10° heading — progress to Shallow Turns (15–20° bank)
  • Build confidence in attitude flying before introducing more complex manoeuvres
  • Maintain emphasis on external lookout throughout all progression
