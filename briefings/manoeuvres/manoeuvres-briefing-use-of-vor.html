05 · Motivation

The VOR remains the most widely available ground-based radio navigation aid in Europe and is a core skill for VFR cross-country and IFR operations alike. While GPS has largely replaced VOR as primary navigation, every professional pilot must be capable of VOR operations as a backup, for procedural approaches, and for airways navigation. The TUNE-SELECT-IDENT discipline instilled in this lesson is the same one used in glass-cockpit airliners. Mastering the discipline early prevents bad habits that are costly to correct later.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Skipping IDENT — using unverified VOR signal (may be maintenance/off-air)
  • Chasing the needle — making continuous small corrections instead of stable WCA
  • Not understanding TO/FROM — misreading position relative to station
  • Flying the wrong OBS setting for the desired course
  • Not applying a lead point on intercept — overshooting the radial
  • Confusing CDI deflection direction when on FROM indication
  • Neglecting aircraft control while head-down on avionics

Theory VOR — How It Works
▸ Principle of Operation
  • VOR = VHF Omnidirectional Range — operates 108.0–117.95 MHz band
  • Ground station emits two signals: reference (omnidirectional) and variable (rotating). Phase difference between the two defines magnetic bearing from station
  • Aircraft receiver measures phase difference → displays bearing as CDI deflection against OBS-selected course
  • TO/FROM flag: TO = flying toward station on selected course; FROM = flying away from station
  • CDI centred + TO flag = aircraft is on the selected inbound radial
SOP Always TUNE → SELECT → IDENT before using any VOR for navigation § 1.22.6
▸ Service Volume & Limitations
  • T-VOR (Terminal): 25 NM / up to 12,000 ft — for approach use only
  • L-VOR (Low altitude): 40 NM / up to 18,000 ft
  • H-VOR (High altitude): 130 NM / 18,000–45,000 ft
  • Line of sight: signal blocked by terrain — unreliable below 1000 ft AGL near station
  • Cone of silence: directly overhead station (±5° cone) — flag may oscillate/disappear
  • NOTAM check: VOR maintenance causes off-air periods — always check before use
02
Setup
TUNE · SELECT · IDENT procedure · OBS setting · position determination
Procedure VOR Tune, Select, Ident & Position Determination
Key Principle
TUNE → SELECT → IDENT is a mandatory sequence — never use a VOR without confirming the Morse code ident. An unidentified VOR may be on maintenance, off-air, or transmitting a false signal.
▸ TUNE — SELECT — IDENT
  1. TUNE: Enter VOR frequency in NAV radio (e.g. 112.50 for ODE). Verify frequency displayed in active window.
  2. SELECT: Set OBS (Omni Bearing Selector) to the desired course (inbound or outbound radial). Verify TO/FROM flag appears.
  3. IDENT: Listen for Morse code identifier (--- -.. for ODE = "ODE"). Confirm within 5 seconds. Call out: "Station ident confirmed — ODE"
▸ Determining Aircraft Position
  1. Rotate OBS until CDI centres with TO flag → read OBS — this is the bearing TO the station (reciprocal = radial you are on).
  2. Rotate OBS until CDI centres with FROM flag → read OBS — this is the radial you are on (bearing FROM station).
  3. Cross-reference radial from two VORs or VOR + fix → plot position on chart.
FI Note
Have the student practice the TUNE-SELECT-IDENT sequence on the ground during the pre-flight, then again in the air before use. Common error: tuning the correct frequency but not waiting for IDENT before starting to navigate. Reinforce: "If you haven't heard the dots and dashes, you haven't tuned it."

Execution
Radial interception · tracking · wind correction · station passage
Procedure VOR Radial Interception — 60° Standard Angle
Key Principle
Standard intercept angle = 60° to the desired course. Do NOT use 90° (overshoots) or 30° (takes too long). Lead the turn: start turning when CDI has approximately 2 dots to go, using the lead angles below.
▸ Intercept Procedure (Inbound)
  1. TUNE-SELECT-IDENT the VOR. Set OBS to desired inbound course.
  2. Determine which side of the radial the aircraft is on — note CDI deflection direction.
  3. Turn aircraft to intercept heading = desired course ± 60° (toward CDI deflection).
  4. Monitor CDI. Call out: "Needle alive" when CDI begins moving.
  5. Apply lead point — begin turn to WCH when CDI reaches: 60° intercept = ~20° from centre, 90° = ~30°, 30° = ~10°.
  6. Roll out on WCH with CDI centred and TO flag showing. Call: "Established inbound — ODE — [radial]"
▸ Tracking on Radial (Wind Correction)
  1. If CDI deflects right: aircraft drifting left — turn right 10°. Hold for 60 sec.
  2. Assess CDI movement: if returning to centre, WCA is correct. If continuing drift, add another 5°.
  3. When CDI recentres: reduce correction by half. Hold new WCH — this is your estimated WCA.
  4. Maintain WCH with CDI centred — do not chase small deflections. Stabilise first, then adjust.
SOP Turn to WCH when needle centres — do not overshoot by continuing on intercept heading past centre § 1.22.6
Theory Station Passage & Outbound Tracking
▸ Station Passage
  • As aircraft overflies station: CDI becomes erratic, TO/FROM flag oscillates or blanks
  • Call out: "Station passage — [time]"
  • Note time — use for position reporting and ETE calculations outbound
  • After passage: TO/FROM flips to FROM — reset OBS to outbound course (reciprocal)
  • Brief cone of silence: CDI reliable approximately 30 sec after passage at normal speeds
▸ Outbound Tracking
  • Set OBS to outbound course (FROM radial). CDI mechanics are the same — needle flies toward the course.
  • Wind correction technique identical to inbound — bracket then halve corrections
  • FROM indication is normal outbound — do not confuse with nav error
  • Monitor elapsed time — use for fuel estimation and ETA calculation
⚠️
Reverse Sensing
If flying outbound on a FROM course with incorrect OBS set to the inbound course — CDI will show reverse deflection. Flying toward the needle will take you off course. Always verify TO/FROM flag matches your direction of flight before making corrections.

04
Check
Assessment standards · debrief guide · knowledge questions · progression
Standards Performance Standards (SOP §1.22.6)
▸ Minimum Acceptable Performance
  • Completes TUNE-SELECT-IDENT without prompting, within 60 seconds of instruction
  • Sets correct OBS for desired course and states inbound/outbound intent
  • Intercepts radial within ±1 CDI dot of centre using 60° angle
  • Applies WCA to maintain track — does not chase needle continuously
  • Identifies station passage and calls time correctly
  • Maintains Alt ±100 ft and S&L flight throughout nav exercise
▸ Progression Decision
  • Consolidation (repeat): fails IDENT check, cannot determine position, reverses CDI corrections, loses aircraft control during head-down work
  • Progress (continue): consistent TUNE-SELECT-IDENT discipline, clean intercept within tolerance, stable tracking demonstrated
Debrief Structured Debrief Guide
1
Walk through your TUNE-SELECT-IDENT sequence — at which step did you verify the station was usable?
2
Describe how you determined which side of the radial you were on before the intercept. What did the CDI tell you?
3
On the intercept, when did you start your turn-on? Did you overshoot or undershoot the radial?
4
What wind correction did you settle on? How did you arrive at it — bracket and halve, or did you guess?
5
What happened at station passage? How did you recognise it, and what did you do immediately after?
Review End-of-Lesson Knowledge Check
  • What does the TO/FROM flag indicate? When does it switch?
  • What is the standard interception angle for a VOR radial?
  • How do you calculate the lead point for a 60° intercept?
  • What causes reverse sensing and how do you avoid it?
  • What is the cone of silence? How do you recognise it?
  • What frequency band does VOR operate on?
  • Why must you always listen to the IDENT before using a VOR?
  • Describe the bracket-and-halve wind correction technique.
