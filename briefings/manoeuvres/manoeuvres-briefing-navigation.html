  • Completed DGH 01–12 — capable of independent S&L, circuits, basic radio navigation

03 · Lesson Objectives
  • Brief the planned route to FI: tracks, headings, ETAs, turning points, altitudes, and alternates
  • Maintain track using Watch–Map–Ground technique and finger-in-map principle
  • Execute the Seven T's correctly at each turning point (Turn, Time, Throttle, Table, Talk, Technical, Track)

  • Maintain Alt ±150 ft, HDG ±15°, IAS +10/−5 kt throughout cruise

05 · Motivation

Navigation is where a student pilot becomes a pilot. The skills learned here — methodical pre-flight planning, disciplined in-flight timing, structured turning-point procedures, and continuous situational awareness — form the foundation of all cross-country flight. A pilot who can navigate reliably on a clear day using only a chart and a stopwatch has the mental framework to handle GPS failures, diversions, and reduced visibility safely. The PLOG and Seven T's are not bureaucratic exercises — they are the thinking structure that keeps you ahead of the aircraft.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Not starting the timer at the turning point — timing errors compound across all legs
  • Skipping one or more of the Seven T's — "Table" and "Talk" most commonly omitted
  • Looking at the map only — not using Watch–Map–Ground in correct sequence
  • "Seeing what you wish to see" — confirming position from a single feature rather than multiple
  • Falling behind the aircraft — not knowing what the next turning point or reporting point is
  • Not correcting TAS after climb to cruise altitude — groundspeed and ETA calculations wrong
  • Fuel imbalance exceeding 30 L — not managing tank switching during flight

Theory Pre-Flight Planning — PLOG & Route Rules (SOP §1.1)
▸ PLOG Required Components
  • Performance table: elevation, QNH, PA, OAT, DA — for both departure and destination
  • Mass & Balance: empty mass + fuel + crew + baggage. TOM ≤720 kg. C.G. within envelope.
  • T/O distance: AFM data corrected for conditions × 1.25. Must be ≤80% of available TODA.
  • Landing distance: corrected × 1.43. Must be ≤70% of LDA.
  • Fuel plan: taxi (3L) + climb + trip (65% power) + 10% contingency + 45 min final reserve + alternate
  • Weather: TAF/METAR for departure, destination, alternate. Low-level significant weather chart.
▸ Route Planning Rules (SOP §1.1.1)
  • Each flight plan leg ≈ 10 minutes — allows timing discipline at turning points
  • First leg starts from aerodrome centre — independent of runway in use
  • Turning points at the edge of cities — never in the middle (ambiguous)
  • Avoid routing over: large forests, lakes, nature-sensitive areas (1000 ft AGL minimum over these)
  • Coastline passages: add reporting point
  • Maintain gliding altitude throughout — must always be able to land safely from current position
  • Route printed on both charts when transitioning between coverage areas
SOP Student must brief FI on route, alternates, fuel, and weather before departure — not after engine start § 1.1
02
Setup
Student route brief · departure configuration · heading & HDG bug · cruise power setting
Procedure Route Brief & Departure Setup
Key Principle
Stay ahead of the aircraft. At any point in the flight, you must know: where you are, where the next turning point is, how long until you reach it, and what you will do when you get there. If you are not busy, something has been forgotten or you should be preparing for what comes next.
▸ Pre-Departure Student Brief (to FI)
  1. Route overview: departure aerodrome → turning points (name each) → destination → alternate.
  2. For each leg: heading (WCH), distance, groundspeed, ETE, ETO, altitude, ATS unit.
  3. Fuel state: total fuel uplifted, minimum required, endurance.
  4. Weather: departure, destination, alternate — ceilings, visibility, wind, TAF validity.
  5. Critical items: NOTAMs affecting route, restricted airspace, glider/parachute activity.
▸ Departure Configuration
  1. Set HDG bug to Wind Corrected Heading (WCH) for first leg before lining up.
  2. After take-off, climb straight ahead to 500 ft AGL — then commence tracking. Note departure time.
  3. At 1000 ft AGL: set 65% cruise power (1975 RPM). Trim. Begin cruise climb to planned altitude at 80 KIAS.
  4. Level at planned altitude. Retrim. Set cruise power. Set altitude alerter. Start navigation: find first turning point on chart and verify ground track.

Procedure En-Route Navigation — Watch–Map–Ground & Seven T's (SOP §1.14.1)
▸ Watch–Map–Ground Technique
  • Watch: Note time elapsed since last fix — how far should you have travelled?
  • Map: Identify terrain features at expected position on chart (forest edge, town, lake, road junction)
  • Ground: Look out the window and confirm those features are visible below/ahead
  • Keep finger on chart at estimated position — advance it continuously with time
  • Never confirm a fix from one feature alone — require 2–3 matching identifiers
SOP Maintain "finger-in-map" at all times — a gap in position awareness means you are behind the aircraft § 1.14.1

  1. Turn: Look out in turn direction — then turn to next leg heading.
  2. Time: Reset stopwatch — note and record ATO.
  3. Throttle: Adjust power for next leg if required.
  4. Table: Enter ATO, compute difference vs. ETO, calculate RETO for next point.
  5. Talk: Make position report to ATS / AFIS.
  6. Technical: Engine temps/pressures, fuel quantity, annunciator — all normal.
  7. Track: Verify on track — heading correct, next turning point identified on chart.
⚠️
Lost Procedure (SOP §1.23.24)
If uncertain of position: stay calm. Maintain altitude and heading — do not turn randomly. Use Watch–Map–Ground to estimate position. Try: (1) VOR radial/DME fix; (2) QDM from ATS for radar identification; (3) if VMC maintained, divert to nearest identifiable landmark. Squawk 7700 if in distress. Notify ATS of situation early — before fuel becomes critical.

Standards Performance Standards & Debrief (SOP §1.14.1)
▸ Minimum Acceptable Performance
  • Route brief delivered before departure — all legs, ETAs, fuel, alternates covered
  • Maintains Alt ±150 ft, HDG ±15°, IAS +10/−5 kt throughout cruise
  • Executes all Seven T's at each turning point — Table and Talk included
  • Uses Watch–Map–Ground consistently — finger on chart
  • Identifies each turning point before reaching it — not after
  • Makes all ATS position reports at correct points without prompting
▸ Key Debrief Questions
1
At which turning point were you most uncertain of your position? What resolved it?
2
Walk through your Seven T's at the last turning point — which step did you do last?
3
Was your ETA accurate? Where did the time error come from?
4
At any point did you feel "behind the aircraft"? What would you do differently to stay ahead?
Review End-of-Lesson Knowledge Check
  • What are the Seven T's and in what order are they performed?
  • What is the Watch–Map–Ground technique?
  • What is the difference between track and heading?
  • How do you calculate ETA for each turning point?
  • What is the maximum fuel imbalance between tanks?
  • What does ATO − ETO tell you about your groundspeed?
  • List the steps of the lost procedure in order.
  • Where must turning points be placed on the chart, and why?
