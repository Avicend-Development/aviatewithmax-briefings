05 · Motivation

Inadvertent flight into IMC (instrument meteorological conditions) is one of the leading causes of fatal general aviation accidents. A VFR pilot who enters cloud without instrument flying skills typically loses control within 3 minutes. The cross-scan taught in this lesson is the exact same technique used in airline operations — it is not a "backup skill," it is the foundation of all precision flying. Even pilots who never intend to fly IFR must have sufficient instrument skills to recover from unexpected IMC exposure.

06 · Common Mistakes
  • Fixating on one instrument — usually the altimeter — while attitude drifts on AI
  • Not trimming — fighting continuous control pressure prevents effective scan
  • Moving eyes too fast — reading instruments requires a brief dwell (1–2 sec each)
  • Over-correcting altitude deviations with pitch — large inputs cause oscillations
  • Ignoring VSI — failing to catch a developing climb or descent early
  • Losing heading discipline — applying bank without checking AI first
  • Spatial disorientation: trusting body senses over the AI when they conflict

BASIC-T · AI at centre-top is the only instrument showing ATTITUDE directly
▸ AI — Primary Attitude Reference
  • AI shows pitch and bank simultaneously — the only instrument that does both
  • Receives approximately 90% of scan attention in IMC
  • All corrections start with the AI — set attitude, then confirm with supporting instruments
  • Never leave the AI for more than 5 seconds in IMC
▸ Supporting Instruments
  • ALT: confirms altitude — lags AI by 3–5 sec. Check after AI settled.
  • HI: confirms heading — cross-check after wings level.
  • VSI: leading indicator of altitude trend — catch deviations early.
  • ASI: confirms pitch indirectly — IAS rising = nose down, falling = nose up.
  • Ball: coordination — centred at all times.
02
Setup
Pre-foggles check · trim · entry configuration · scan pattern briefing
Procedure Entry Setup & Scan Pattern Introduction
Key Principle
Trim before foggles. The aircraft must be in trim and stable before the student puts on the view-limiting device — an out-of-trim aircraft will drift immediately and the student will be chasing it from the start. Confirm: wings level, altitude stable, correct power set.
▸ Pre-Foggles Setup
  1. Establish S&L at cruise altitude — 2250 RPM, 90 KIAS.
  2. Trim aircraft: release yoke pressure completely — aircraft should maintain attitude hands-off for 5+ seconds.
  3. Brief student on scan cycle: "AI → ALT → HI → VSI → back to AI. Spend at least 1–2 seconds per instrument. AI is home base."
  4. FI brief: "I will monitor outside. You fly on instruments only. Tell me what you see on each instrument as you scan."
  5. Student dons foggles. FI confirms outside clear — commence exercise.
▸ The Cross-Scan Cycle
  1. AI: Check wings level, pitch attitude. Any correction needed? Apply small input.
  2. ALT: On target? Note trend. If off: return to AI and adjust pitch by 1–2° only.
  3. HI: On heading? If off: return to AI, apply bank max 10–15°, then roll level on HI.
  4. VSI: Zero for level? If climbing or descending: note rate, return to AI to correct.
  5. AI: Return — always. This is home base. Repeat cycle every 5–8 seconds.
SOP Trim continuously — as speed or power changes, retrim before resuming the scan § 2.2

03
Execution
S&L under foggles · instrument turns · unusual attitude recovery · common errors
Procedure Instrument Manoeuvres (SOP §2.2)
▸ Straight & Level (Instrument)
  1. Set attitude on AI — wings level, pitch dot on horizon bar.
  2. Begin scan: AI → ALT → HI → VSI → AI. Verbalise each reading aloud.
  3. If altitude deviates: return to AI first. Adjust pitch by 1–2° only. Confirm on ALT after 5 sec.
  4. If heading deviates: AI bank to max 15°. Level when HI shows desired heading −10° (lead). Roll level on AI.
  5. Retrim whenever continuous control pressure felt — trim before returning to full scan.
▸ Instrument Turns (Gentle)
  1. Set bank on AI — target 15° bank. Increase back pressure slightly as bank increases.
  2. Monitor HI for heading progress. Apply lead: at 10–15° before target heading, begin rollout.
  3. Roll level on AI — confirm wings level with AI horizon bar.
  4. Check ALT after roll-out — adjust pitch if needed. Ball centred throughout.
⛔
Unusual Attitude Recovery
If FI introduces unusual attitude (nose-high or nose-low, banked): Wings level first on AI — regardless of nose position. Then pitch: nose-high = lower pitch; nose-low = raise pitch. Do NOT pull back while still banked — this tightens the spiral and increases G. Level wings, THEN pitch.
FI Note
Introduce unusual attitudes gently at first — 30° bank, 10° pitch deviation. Ensure the student verbally identifies the AI reading before recovering. Coach: "What does the AI tell you? Level the wings first." Do NOT allow the student to recover by feel alone — the whole lesson is about replacing body senses with instrument reading.
04
Check
Assessment standards · debrief questions · knowledge check · progression
Standards Performance Standards & Debrief (SOP §2.2)
▸ Minimum Acceptable Performance
  • Maintains Alt ±200 ft and HDG ±20° in S&L under foggles
  • Demonstrates continuous scan — not fixating on single instrument
  • Returns to AI within 5 seconds after each supporting instrument check
  • Trims before full scan — no continuous pressure fighting
  • Recovers unusual attitude: wings level first, then pitch — no all-back-pressure recovery
  • Names all 5 instruments in the Basic-T and their primary roles
▸ Progression Decision
  • Consolidation (repeat): fixates on single instrument, loses 300+ ft in S&L, recovers unusual attitude by feel, refuses to trust AI over body senses
  • Progress (continue): tolerances met, scan pattern established, unusual attitude recovery safe within 2 demonstrations
▸ Key Debrief Questions
1
Which instrument did you spend the most time on? Was that the right choice?
2
In the unusual attitude — what was your first instinct? Did you follow the AI or your body sense?
3
When did you feel the need to trim? How often did you trim overall?
Review End-of-Lesson Knowledge Check
  • What is the Basic-T and which instrument is at its centre?
  • Why must you return to the AI after every other instrument check?
  • What does the VSI tell you that the altimeter does not?
  • What is the maximum time to spend off the AI in IMC?
  • Describe the correct unusual attitude recovery sequence.
  • What does an increasing airspeed tell you about pitch attitude?
  • Why must the aircraft be trimmed before starting the cross-scan?
  • What causes spatial disorientation and how do you counter it?
